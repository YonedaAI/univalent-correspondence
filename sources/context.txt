Great question — and there's no single universally-accepted name, which is itself telling. Let me give you the actual landscape, because the naming reflects unresolved priority disputes and different emphases.

## The names actually in use

**1. The Curry–Howard–Lambek correspondence** (or "CHL")

This is the most established name for the *three-way* version (logic ↔ type theory ↔ category theory). Curry and Howard established the logic-types correspondence; Lambek extended it to cartesian closed categories. This is the textbook name, but it stops short of homotopy.

**2. The Curry–Howard–Lambek–Voevodsky correspondence**

Sometimes used (informally) when the homotopy/topology column is included, crediting Voevodsky for the univalent extension. Not standardized but you'll see it in talks and blog posts.

**3. The "computational trinity"** (Robert Harper's term)

Harper at CMU popularized this name for the three-way version: logic, languages (type theory), categories. It's catchy and you'll see it in his lectures and writings. The four-way version is sometimes called the **"computational tetrad"** or **"computational quaternity"** but these haven't stuck.

**4. The "propositions-as-types" paradigm** (or "propositions as types")

Philip Wadler's preferred framing — emphasizes the central insight rather than the historical figures. His paper *Propositions as Types* (CACM 2015) is the canonical reference. Extends naturally to "propositions as types as spaces" in the HoTT setting.

**5. Univalent Foundations**

Voevodsky's name for the *foundational program* built on the four-way correspondence. This is the closest thing to a name for "the unified language" as a working mathematical framework, not just a correspondence. **Homotopy Type Theory (HoTT)** is the type-theoretic side of Univalent Foundations.

**6. The Brouwer–Heyting–Kolmogorov–Curry–Howard–Lambek–Martin-Löf–Voevodsky correspondence**

Half-joking name occasionally seen, acknowledging that the lineage runs through intuitionistic logic (Brouwer, Heyting, Kolmogorov), the computational interpretation (Curry, Howard), the categorical semantics (Lambek), dependent types (Martin-Löf), and homotopy (Voevodsky). Nobody actually says this out loud, but it's accurate.

## The honest answer

If you're writing a paper and want to be precise:

- **For the three-way correspondence:** "Curry–Howard–Lambek correspondence" is standard.
- **For the four-way correspondence:** there is no canonical name. Most people write something like "the Curry–Howard correspondence extended to homotopy type theory" or "the propositions-as-types-as-spaces paradigm."
- **For the foundational program built on it:** "Univalent Foundations" (the project) or "Homotopy Type Theory" (the formal system).

## What it *should* probably be called

Given your work, the most defensible naming choices are:

1. **The structural correspondence** — emphasizes that all four columns are views of the same underlying structural mathematics. Generic but accurate.

2. **The univalent correspondence** — credits the unifying insight (univalence) that makes all four columns *literally* the same system rather than merely analogous.

3. **The ∞-groupoid correspondence** — names the underlying object that all four columns describe. A type, a proposition, a homotopy type, and a higher category all turn out to be $\infty$-groupoids in the right sense.

4. **The fourfold way** or **tetralogy** — informal but evocative.

The synthesis here is *enabling*, not *complete*.
