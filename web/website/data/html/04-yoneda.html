Abstract

We develop the Yoneda perspective on the question “what is a number?” as the fourth installment of the Univalent Correspondence series. Building on the universal-property characterization of natural numbers as initial successor structures (Paper III), we promote the relational viewpoint to its categorical limit: the Yoneda embedding y:𝒞↪[𝒞op,𝑺𝒆𝒕]y\colon \mathcal{C} \hookrightarrow [\mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{op}}, \mathbf{Set}], X↦Hom𝒞(−,X)X \mapsto \mathrm{Hom}_{\mathcal{C}}(-, X), which is fully faithful for any locally small category 𝒞\mathcal{C}. The corollary X≅Y⇔Hom(−,X)≅Hom(−,Y)X \cong Y \iff \mathrm{Hom}(-, X) \cong \mathrm{Hom}(-, Y) supplies a precise sense in which an object is its system of relationships: the natural number 5858 is the representable functor Hom(−,58)\mathrm{Hom}(-, 58) on the appropriate category. We give the full proof of the Yoneda lemma, exhibit the density theorem expressing every presheaf as a colimit of representables, and apply the framework beyond arithmetic to groups (regular representations), spaces (functor of points), and schemes (Grothendieck’s program). Our analytic applications include π\pi as the representing object for periods of the exponential map ℝ→S1\mathbb{R} \to S^{1} and ee as the unique element making exp\exp a continuous group homomorphism (ℝ,+)→(ℝ>0,⋅)(\mathbb{R}, +) \to (\mathbb{R}^{>0}, \cdot). We outline the enriched, 22-categorical, and ∞\infty-categorical generalizations of Yoneda, culminating in Lurie’s ∞\infty-Yoneda lemma. We close by linking Yoneda to univalence: under the Univalent Foundations program, the equivalence Hom(−,X)≅Hom(−,Y)⇒X=Y\mathrm{Hom}(-, X) \cong \mathrm{Hom}(-, Y) \Rightarrow X = Y is internalized as a path in the universe, identifying the Yoneda principle with the univalence axiom and preparing Paper V (HoTT). A companion Haskell implementation in src/04-yoneda/ computes Yoneda embeddings of finite categories and exhibits Hom(−,n)\mathrm{Hom}(-, n) for finite initial segments of ℕ\mathbb{N} as a poset.

Introduction

The arc so far

The series The Univalent Correspondence examines a single question — what is the natural number 5858? — through six progressively refined formalisms. Paper I (Naive) treated 5858 as a syntactic symbol and exposed the Frege-style sense/reference confusion that results from identifying numerals with numbers. Paper II (Set-Theoretic) gave the von Neumann encoding 58={0,1,…,57}58 = \{0, 1, \ldots, 57\} and exhibited Benacerraf’s identification problem: alternative encodings (Zermelo’s, Quine’s, etc.) are equally valid, so no single set-theoretic representative deserves the title the natural numbers. Paper III (Universal Property) responded to Benacerraf by characterizing ℕ\mathbb{N} up to canonical isomorphism as the initial (0,succ)(0, \mathrm{succ})-algebra, equivalently as the natural number object (NNO) in any category with a terminal object. The number 5858 became any canonical 5858th iterate of succ\mathrm{succ} in any structure satisfying the universal property: well-defined up to unique isomorphism, encoding-free.

The present paper takes the next step on the structuralist ladder. The universal-property viewpoint already speaks of objects through their morphisms in and out, but it does so one universal property at a time. The Yoneda perspective globalizes this principle. Rather than say “ℕ\mathbb{N} is initial among successor structures,” it says: every object XX in a locally small category 𝒞\mathcal{C} is fully reconstructible from its profile Hom𝒞(−,X)\mathrm{Hom}_{\mathcal{C}}(-, X) as a functor 𝒞op→𝑺𝒆𝒕\mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{op}} \to \mathbf{Set}. The Yoneda lemma promotes the slogan “an object is determined by its relationships” from heuristic to theorem.

The Yoneda principle in one sentence

An object is its system of probes.

Formally, the Yoneda lemma asserts a natural bijection Nat(Hom𝒞(−,X),F)≅F(X)\mathrm{Nat}\big(\mathrm{Hom}_{\mathcal{C}}(-, X), F\big) \;\cong\; F(X) for any presheaf F:𝒞op→𝑺𝒆𝒕F\colon \mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{op}} \to \mathbf{Set} and object X∈𝒞X \in \mathcal{C}. Specializing F=Hom𝒞(−,Y)F = \mathrm{Hom}_{\mathcal{C}}(-, Y) recovers the embedding statement Nat(Hom(−,X),Hom(−,Y))≅Hom𝒞(X,Y),\mathrm{Nat}\big(\mathrm{Hom}(-, X), \mathrm{Hom}(-, Y)\big) \;\cong\; \mathrm{Hom}_{\mathcal{C}}(X, Y), so the assignment y:X↦Hom(−,X)y\colon X \mapsto \mathrm{Hom}(-, X) is fully faithful. Two objects whose profiles agree as functors are isomorphic; two morphisms that induce the same natural transformation are equal.

Why this matters for “what is 5858?”

In the category 𝑵𝑵𝑶\mathbf{NNO} of natural-number objects in 𝑺𝒆𝒕\mathbf{Set} (or in any topos with NNO), the Yoneda embedding identifies 5858 not with a particular set, term, or numeral, but with the functor that assigns to every successor structure (X,x0,f)(X, x_0, f) the value f58(x0)f^{58}(x_0). This is the content of Section 4. The structuralist’s slogan — numbers are positions in a structure — becomes a theorem: 5858 is the unique-up-to-isomorphism object representing the functor that probes structures for “5858th iterate of the successor map starting at the basepoint.”

Bridge to univalence

The Yoneda lemma yields equivalence of objects from equivalence of their representable functors. Univalence (Paper V) internalizes this implication: the canonical map (A=B)→(A≃B)(A = B) \to (A \simeq B) is itself an equivalence in Homotopy Type Theory. Yoneda produces an equivalence; univalence declares it an identity. Section 8 makes this connection precise and motivates the next paper.

Mathematical Framework

We assume the reader is fluent with category theory at the level of Mac Lane  or Riehl . We fix terminology and notation in this section.

Categories and functors

Definition 1 (Locally small category). A category 𝒞\mathcal{C} is locally small when for each pair of objects X,Y∈Ob(𝒞)X, Y \in \mathrm{Ob}(\mathcal{C}) the collection Hom𝒞(X,Y)\mathrm{Hom}_{\mathcal{C}}(X, Y) is a set (rather than a proper class). All categories considered in this paper are locally small unless explicitly stated otherwise.

Definition 2 (Presheaf). Given a category 𝒞\mathcal{C}, a presheaf on 𝒞\mathcal{C} is a functor F:𝒞op→𝑺𝒆𝒕F\colon \mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{op}} \to \mathbf{Set}. The category of presheaves and natural transformations is denoted 𝒞̂:=[𝒞op,𝑺𝒆𝒕]\widehat{\mathcal{C}} := [\mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{op}}, \mathbf{Set}].

Definition 3 (Representable functor). A presheaf F:𝒞op→𝑺𝒆𝒕F\colon \mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{op}} \to \mathbf{Set} is representable if there exist an object X∈𝒞X \in \mathcal{C} and a natural isomorphism η:Hom𝒞(−,X)→≅F\eta\colon \mathrm{Hom}_{\mathcal{C}}(-, X) \xrightarrow{\;\cong\;} F. The pair (X,η)(X, \eta) is called a representation; we say XX represents FF.

The Yoneda embedding

Definition 4 (Yoneda embedding). The Yoneda embedding is the functor y:𝒞→𝒞̂,X↦Hom𝒞(−,X),y\colon \mathcal{C} \longrightarrow \widehat{\mathcal{C}},\qquad X \longmapsto \mathrm{Hom}_{\mathcal{C}}(-, X), sending a morphism f:X→Yf\colon X \to Y to the natural transformation y(f)y(f) from Hom(−,X)\mathrm{Hom}(-, X) to Hom(−,Y)\mathrm{Hom}(-, Y) whose component at an object ZZ is post-composition by ff: y(f)Z=f∘−:Hom(Z,X)→Hom(Z,Y).y(f)_Z \;=\; f \circ -\;\colon\; \mathrm{Hom}(Z, X) \longrightarrow \mathrm{Hom}(Z, Y).

We frequently write hX:=Hom𝒞(−,X)h_X := \mathrm{Hom}_{\mathcal{C}}(-, X). The dual (covariant) Yoneda embedding is y′:𝒞op→[𝒞,𝑺𝒆𝒕],X↦Hom𝒞(X,−)=:hX.y'\colon \mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{op}} \longrightarrow [\mathcal{C}, \mathbf{Set}],\qquad X \longmapsto \mathrm{Hom}_{\mathcal{C}}(X, -) =: h^{X}.

Diagrammatic conventions

We display natural transformations as squares $$\begin{tikzcd} F(Z) \arrow[r, "\alpha_Z"] \arrow[d, "F(g)"'] & G(Z) \arrow[d, "G(g)"] \\ F(W) \arrow[r, "\alpha_W"'] & G(W) \end{tikzcd}$$ which commute for every g:W→Zg\colon W \to Z. For presheaves, gg goes against the arrows of 𝒞\mathcal{C}; we silently use 𝒞op\mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{op}}.

The Yoneda Lemma

Statement and proof

Theorem 5 (Yoneda lemma). Let 𝒞\mathcal{C} be a locally small category, X∈𝒞X \in \mathcal{C} an object, and F:𝒞op→𝑺𝒆𝒕F\colon \mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{op}} \to \mathbf{Set} a presheaf. There is a bijection ΦX,F:Nat(hX,F)→≅F(X),α↦αX(idX),\Phi_{X,F}\colon \mathrm{Nat}\big(h_X, F\big) \xrightarrow{\;\cong\;} F(X), \qquad \alpha \longmapsto \alpha_X(\mathrm{id}_X), natural in both XX and FF.

Proof. We construct an inverse. Given u∈F(X)u \in F(X), define Ψ(u):hX→F\Psi(u)\colon h_X \to F component-wise by Ψ(u)Z:Hom𝒞(Z,X)→F(Z),f↦F(f)(u).\Psi(u)_Z\colon \mathrm{Hom}_{\mathcal{C}}(Z, X) \to F(Z),\qquad f \longmapsto F(f)(u). This is natural: for g:W→Zg\colon W \to Z, the square $$\begin{tikzcd} \mathrm{Hom}(Z, X) \arrow[r, "\Psi(u)_Z"] \arrow[d, "- \circ g"'] & F(Z) \arrow[d, "F(g)"] \\ \mathrm{Hom}(W, X) \arrow[r, "\Psi(u)_W"'] & F(W) \end{tikzcd}$$ commutes by functoriality of FF: Ψ(u)W(f∘g)=F(f∘g)(u)=F(g)(F(f)(u))=F(g)(Ψ(u)Z(f)).\Psi(u)_W(f \circ g) \;=\; F(f \circ g)(u) \;=\; F(g)(F(f)(u)) \;=\; F(g)(\Psi(u)_Z(f)).

We check Φ∘Ψ=idF(X)\Phi \circ \Psi = \mathrm{id}_{F(X)}: (Φ∘Ψ)(u)=Ψ(u)X(idX)=F(idX)(u)=idF(X)(u)=u.(\Phi \circ \Psi)(u) = \Psi(u)_X(\mathrm{id}_X) = F(\mathrm{id}_X)(u) = \mathrm{id}_{F(X)}(u) = u. Conversely, Ψ∘Φ=idNat(hX,F)\Psi \circ \Phi = \mathrm{id}_{\mathrm{Nat}(h_X, F)}: for α:hX→F\alpha\colon h_X \to F and f:Z→Xf\colon Z \to X, Ψ(Φ(α))Z(f)=Ψ(αX(idX))Z(f)=F(f)(αX(idX))=αZ(idX∘f)=αZ(f),\Psi(\Phi(\alpha))_Z(f) = \Psi(\alpha_X(\mathrm{id}_X))_Z(f) = F(f)(\alpha_X(\mathrm{id}_X)) = \alpha_Z(\mathrm{id}_X \circ f) = \alpha_Z(f), where the third equality uses naturality of α\alpha applied to f:Z→Xf\colon Z \to X, viewed as a morphism Z→XZ \to X in 𝒞\mathcal{C} (equivalently X→ZX \to Z in 𝒞op\mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{op}}).

Naturality of Φ\Phi in XX: for g:X→X′g\colon X \to X' and α:hX′→F\alpha\colon h_{X'} \to F, the diagram $$\begin{tikzcd} \mathrm{Nat}(h_{X'}, F) \arrow[r, "\Phi_{X', F}"] \arrow[d, "(- \circ y(g))"'] & F(X') \arrow[d, "F(g)"] \\ \mathrm{Nat}(h_X, F) \arrow[r, "\Phi_{X, F}"'] & F(X) \end{tikzcd}$$ commutes by naturality of α\alpha at g:X→X′g\colon X \to X' (viewed in 𝒞\mathcal{C}, hence backwards in 𝒞op\mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{op}}). Going right-then-down yields F(g)(αX′(idX′))F(g)(\alpha_{X'}(\mathrm{id}_{X'})). Going down-then-right yields (α∘y(g))X(idX)=αX(g∘idX)=αX(g)(\alpha \circ y(g))_{X}(\mathrm{id}_X) = \alpha_X(g \circ \mathrm{id}_X) = \alpha_X(g). These agree because the naturality square of α\alpha at gg states αX∘hX′(g)=F(g)∘αX′,\alpha_X \circ h_{X'}(g) = F(g) \circ \alpha_{X'}, and evaluating both sides at idX′∈hX′(X′)\mathrm{id}_{X'} \in h_{X'}(X') gives αX(g)=F(g)(αX′(idX′))\alpha_X(g) = F(g)(\alpha_{X'}(\mathrm{id}_{X'})). Naturality in FF is similar (and immediate from the definition of Φ\Phi as evaluation at idX\mathrm{id}_X). ◻

Remark 6 (The element αX(idX)\alpha_X(\mathrm{id}_X)). The proof reveals the algorithmic content of Yoneda. A natural transformation out of hXh_X is determined by where it sends the universal element idX∈hX(X)=Hom(X,X)\mathrm{id}_X \in h_X(X) = \mathrm{Hom}(X, X). Every other component is forced by naturality.

The embedding theorem

Corollary 7 (Fully faithful Yoneda embedding). Specializing F=hYF = h_Y in 5, Nat(hX,hY)≅hY(X)=Hom𝒞(X,Y).\mathrm{Nat}(h_X, h_Y) \;\cong\; h_Y(X) \;=\; \mathrm{Hom}_{\mathcal{C}}(X, Y). The Yoneda embedding y:𝒞→𝒞̂y\colon \mathcal{C} \to \widehat{\mathcal{C}} is fully faithful.

Proof. The bijection of 5 sends a natural transformation α:hX→hY\alpha\colon h_X \to h_Y to the element αX(idX)∈Hom(X,Y)\alpha_X(\mathrm{id}_X) \in \mathrm{Hom}(X, Y), and inversely sends f:X→Yf\colon X \to Y to y(f)y(f). Faithfulness is injectivity of yy on hom-sets; fullness is surjectivity. Both follow from this bijection. ◻

Corollary 8 (Yoneda’s identity criterion). For X,Y∈𝒞X, Y \in \mathcal{C}, X≅Y⇔hX≅hY as presheaves.X \cong Y \iff h_X \cong h_Y \text{ as presheaves.}

Proof. A fully faithful functor reflects and preserves isomorphisms. ◻

The contravariant (dual) form

Theorem 9 (Covariant Yoneda). For X∈𝒞X \in \mathcal{C} and G:𝒞→𝑺𝒆𝒕G\colon \mathcal{C} \to \mathbf{Set}, there is a natural bijection Nat(hX,G)≅G(X),β↦βX(idX).\mathrm{Nat}(h^X, G) \cong G(X), \qquad \beta \longmapsto \beta_X(\mathrm{id}_X). The covariant Yoneda embedding y′:𝒞op→[𝒞,𝑺𝒆𝒕]y'\colon \mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{op}} \to [\mathcal{C}, \mathbf{Set}], X↦Hom𝒞(X,−)X \mapsto \mathrm{Hom}_{\mathcal{C}}(X, -), is fully faithful.

Proof. Apply 5 to 𝒞op\mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{op}} and unfold definitions. Components are now precomposition rather than postcomposition. ◻

The two forms answer two questions about an object XX:

  • hX=Hom(−,X)h_X = \mathrm{Hom}(-, X): “which objects map into XX, and how?” — the generalized elements of XX.

  • hX=Hom(X,−)h^X = \mathrm{Hom}(X, -): “which objects does XX map into, and how?” — the co-elements or co-probes.

Both encode XX up to isomorphism.

The Yoneda lemma as universality

Proposition 10 (Universal element). Let F:𝒞op→𝑺𝒆𝒕F\colon \mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{op}} \to \mathbf{Set} be a presheaf. A representation of FF by XX is the same data as a universal element u∈F(X)u \in F(X): an element such that for every Z∈𝒞Z \in \mathcal{C} and v∈F(Z)v \in F(Z) there exists a unique f:Z→Xf\colon Z \to X with F(f)(u)=vF(f)(u) = v.

Proof. Given representation η:hX→≅F\eta\colon h_X \xrightarrow{\cong} F, set u:=ηX(idX)u := \eta_X(\mathrm{id}_X); the inverse η−1:F(Z)→hX(Z)=Hom(Z,X)\eta^{-1}\colon F(Z) \to h_X(Z) = \mathrm{Hom}(Z, X) sends vv to the unique ff with F(f)(u)=vF(f)(u) = v, by the explicit form η=Ψ(u)\eta = \Psi(u) of 5. Conversely, a universal element induces Ψ(u)\Psi(u) which is invertible by uniqueness. ◻

This is the bridge to Paper III: every universal property is the assertion that some presheaf is representable.

Numbers as Representable Functors

We now apply the Yoneda machinery to the central question of the series: what is a number?

The category of pointed endomorphism structures

Definition 11 (Pointed endomorphism structure). A pointed endomorphism structure, or successor structure, is a triple (X,x0,f)(X, x_0, f) where XX is a set, x0∈Xx_0 \in X a basepoint, and f:X→Xf\colon X \to X an endomorphism. A morphism (X,x0,f)→(Y,y0,g)(X, x_0, f) \to (Y, y_0, g) is a function φ:X→Y\varphi\colon X \to Y with φ(x0)=y0\varphi(x_0) = y_0 and φ∘f=g∘φ\varphi \circ f = g \circ \varphi. Let 𝑺𝒖𝒄𝒄\mathbf{Succ} denote the resulting category.

By Lawvere’s NNO theorem (Paper III), (ℕ,0,succ)(\mathbb{N}, 0, \mathrm{succ}) is initial in 𝑺𝒖𝒄𝒄\mathbf{Succ}.

Recasting numbers as functors

Definition 12 (The underlying-set functor on 𝑺𝒖𝒄𝒄\mathbf{Succ}). Let U:𝑺𝒖𝒄𝒄→𝑺𝒆𝒕U\colon \mathbf{Succ} \to \mathbf{Set} be the forgetful functor sending (X,x0,f)↦X(X, x_0, f) \mapsto X and φ↦φ\varphi \mapsto \varphi (the underlying set-function). Let 𝟏:𝑺𝒖𝒄𝒄→𝑺𝒆𝒕\mathbf{1}\colon \mathbf{Succ} \to \mathbf{Set} denote the constant functor at the singleton {*}\{*\}. A natural transformation 𝟏⇒U\mathbf{1} \Rightarrow U is exactly a coherent choice of element ηO∈U(O)\eta_{O} \in U(O) for each O∈𝑺𝒖𝒄𝒄O \in \mathbf{Succ} such that for every morphism φ:O→O′\varphi\colon O \to O', U(φ)(ηO)=ηO′U(\varphi)(\eta_{O}) = \eta_{O'}.

Definition 13 (The nnth-iterate selector). For each n∈ℕn \in \mathbb{N}, define the nnth-iterate selector as the natural transformation εn:𝟏⇒U,εn(X,x0,f)(*):=fn(x0)∈X=U(X,x0,f).\varepsilon_n\colon \mathbf{1} \Longrightarrow U,\qquad \varepsilon_n^{(X, x_0, f)}(*) := f^n(x_0) \in X = U(X, x_0, f). Naturality: for φ:(X,x0,f)→(Y,y0,g)\varphi\colon (X, x_0, f) \to (Y, y_0, g) in 𝑺𝒖𝒄𝒄\mathbf{Succ}, the equations φ∘f=g∘φ\varphi \circ f = g \circ \varphi and φ(x0)=y0\varphi(x_0) = y_0 give inductively φ(fn(x0))=gn(y0)\varphi(f^n(x_0)) = g^n(y_0), i.e., U(φ)(εn(X,x0,f)(*))=εn(Y,y0,g)(*)U(\varphi)(\varepsilon_n^{(X, x_0, f)}(*)) = \varepsilon_n^{(Y, y_0, g)}(*).

The functor UU is the relational “probe”: it asks of each successor structure “what is your underlying set?” The natural transformations εn:𝟏⇒U\varepsilon_n\colon \mathbf{1} \Rightarrow U then pick out a distinguished element of each underlying set.

The representability theorem

Theorem 14 (Numbers as natural transformations). There is a natural bijection Nat(𝟏,U)≅ℕ\mathrm{Nat}\!\big(\mathbf{1}, U\big) \;\cong\; \mathbb{N} between natural transformations from the constant-singleton functor 𝟏:𝑺𝒖𝒄𝒄→𝑺𝒆𝒕\mathbf{1}\colon \mathbf{Succ} \to \mathbf{Set} to the underlying-set functor U:𝑺𝒖𝒄𝒄→𝑺𝒆𝒕U\colon \mathbf{Succ} \to \mathbf{Set}, and natural numbers. The bijection sends εn↦n\varepsilon_n \mapsto n.

Proof. By initiality of (ℕ,0,succ)(\mathbb{N}, 0, \mathrm{succ}) in 𝑺𝒖𝒄𝒄\mathbf{Succ}, the corepresentable hom-functor h(ℕ,0,succ):=Hom𝑺𝒖𝒄𝒄((ℕ,0,succ),−):𝑺𝒖𝒄𝒄→𝑺𝒆𝒕h^{(\mathbb{N}, 0, \mathrm{succ})} := \mathrm{Hom}_{\mathbf{Succ}}\!\big((\mathbb{N}, 0, \mathrm{succ}), -\big)\colon \mathbf{Succ} \longrightarrow \mathbf{Set} sends every object to a singleton. Hence it is canonically naturally isomorphic to the constant-singleton functor 𝟏\mathbf{1}. By the covariant Yoneda lemma (9) applied to h(ℕ,0,succ)h^{(\mathbb{N}, 0, \mathrm{succ})} and to UU, Nat(h(ℕ,0,succ),U)≅U(ℕ,0,succ)=ℕ.\mathrm{Nat}\!\big(h^{(\mathbb{N}, 0, \mathrm{succ})}, U\big) \;\cong\; U(\mathbb{N}, 0, \mathrm{succ}) \;=\; \mathbb{N}. Composing with the canonical isomorphism 𝟏≅h(ℕ,0,succ)\mathbf{1} \cong h^{(\mathbb{N}, 0, \mathrm{succ})} gives Nat(𝟏,U)≅ℕ.\mathrm{Nat}\!\big(\mathbf{1}, U\big) \;\cong\; \mathbb{N}. Concretely the bijection sends η:𝟏⇒U\eta\colon \mathbf{1} \Rightarrow U to η(ℕ,0,succ)(*)∈ℕ\eta_{(\mathbb{N}, 0, \mathrm{succ})}(*) \in \mathbb{N}. Applied to η=εn\eta = \varepsilon_n, we get εn(ℕ,0,succ)(*)=succn(0)=n\varepsilon_n^{(\mathbb{N}, 0, \mathrm{succ})}(*) = \mathrm{succ}^n(0) = n, so εn↦n\varepsilon_n \mapsto n. Conversely, given n∈ℕn \in \mathbb{N}, naturality forces the component at (X,x0,f)(X, x_0, f) to be U(rec(X,x0,f))(n)=fn(x0)U(\mathrm{rec}_{(X, x_0, f)})(n) = f^n(x_0), recovering εn\varepsilon_n. ◻

Remark 15 (The role of the initial structure). The initial object (ℕ,0,succ)(\mathbb{N}, 0, \mathrm{succ}) corepresents the constant-singleton functor 𝟏\mathbf{1}, not UU. Calling ℕ\mathbb{N} a corepresenting object of UU would conflict with the standard meaning: the corepresentable h(ℕ,0,succ)=Hom𝑺𝒖𝒄𝒄((ℕ,0,succ),−)h^{(\mathbb{N}, 0, \mathrm{succ})} = \mathrm{Hom}_{\mathbf{Succ}}\!\big((\mathbb{N}, 0, \mathrm{succ}), -\big) is constant on singletons, while UU takes generally non-singleton values.

The role of initiality in 14 is more subtle. Initiality provides the canonical identification 𝟏≅h(ℕ,0,succ)\mathbf{1} \cong h^{(\mathbb{N}, 0, \mathrm{succ})}, which together with the covariant Yoneda lemma yields the bijection Nat(𝟏,U)≅U(ℕ,0,succ)=ℕ\mathrm{Nat}(\mathbf{1}, U) \cong U(\mathbb{N}, 0, \mathrm{succ}) = \mathbb{N}. The natural number nn then is the natural transformation εn\varepsilon_n that picks out fn(x0)f^n(x_0) in every successor structure — the Yoneda formulation of primitive recursion.

Numbers as objects of (ℕ,≤)(\mathbb{N}, \le)

14 encodes each n∈ℕn \in \mathbb{N} as a natural transformation on 𝑺𝒖𝒄𝒄\mathbf{Succ}, but it does not display nn as the representing object of any single representable presheaf. To realize each individual nn as its own representable functor we descend to a category whose objects are the natural numbers themselves. The order category (ℕ,≤)(\mathbb{N}, \le) does this:

Proposition 16 (Identity criterion in (ℕ,≤)(\mathbb{N}, \le)). View (ℕ,≤)(\mathbb{N}, \le) as a thin category with one morphism m→nm \to n when m≤nm \le n. The Yoneda embedding y:(ℕ,≤)↪(ℕ,≤)̂y\colon (\mathbb{N}, \le) \hookrightarrow \widehat{(\mathbb{N}, \le)} sends nn to the presheaf hn(m)={{*}m≤n,∅m>n.h_{n}(m) = \begin{cases} \{*\} & m \le n, \\ \emptyset & m > n.\end{cases} Two natural numbers n,n′n, n' satisfy hn≅hn′h_n \cong h_{n'} if and only if n=n′n = n'.

Proof. The presheaf hnh_n has support {0,1,…,n}\{0, 1, \ldots, n\}. Two presheaves with non-empty support agree if and only if their supports do, i.e., n=n′n = n'. This is 8 specialized to a thin category, where “isomorphic” reduces to “equal”. ◻

Remark 17 (Yoneda gloss in (ℕ,≤)(\mathbb{N}, \le)). The slogan “5858 is Hom(−,58)\mathrm{Hom}(-, 58)” now has formal content within (ℕ,≤)(\mathbb{N}, \le): the object 5858 is identified with the presheaf h58h_{58} whose support is exactly {0,1,…,58}\{0, 1, \ldots, 58\}. By the identity criterion, 5858 is the unique object with this presheaf. The two formalizations — 𝑺𝒖𝒄𝒄\mathbf{Succ} in 14 and (ℕ,≤)(\mathbb{N}, \le) in 16 — are complementary in the following sense: the former characterizes the entire structure of natural numbers as a single relational invariant (via natural transformations 𝟏⇒U\mathbf{1} \Rightarrow U) across all possible successor systems, while the latter individuates each number nn by its unique relational “history” (the support presheaf hnh_n) within the ordered set of natural numbers themselves.

Beyond order: the categorical structure of 5858

The order category (ℕ,≤)(\mathbb{N}, \le) is rich enough for individuation but too rigid to capture all arithmetic structure. A richer category is the Lawvere theory ℒnat\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{nat}} of natural numbers, whose objects are natural numbers 0,1,2,…0, 1, 2, \ldots and whose morphisms Hom(m,n)\mathrm{Hom}(m, n) are functions {0,…,m−1}→{0,…,n−1}\{0, \ldots, m-1\} \to \{0, \ldots, n-1\}.

Proposition 18 (Yoneda for ℒnat\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{nat}}). In ℒnat\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{nat}}, the representable functor hn=Hom(−,n)h_n = \mathrm{Hom}(-, n) assigns to each object mm the hom-set Homℒnat(m,n)\mathrm{Hom}_{\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{nat}}}(m, n) — i.e., the set of all functions {0,…,m−1}→{0,…,n−1}\{0, \ldots, m-1\} \to \{0, \ldots, n-1\} — of cardinality |hn(m)|=nm|h_n(m)| = n^m. Two objects n,n′n, n' are isomorphic in ℒnat\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{nat}} iff n=n′n = n', equivalently iff |hn(m)|=|hn′(m)||h_n(m)| = |h_{n'}(m)| for all mm.

Proof. |hn(m)|=nm|h_n(m)| = n^m. For each fixed m≥1m \ge 1, the value nmn^m determines nn. The isomorphism criterion (n≅n′⇔hn≅hn′n \cong n' \iff h_n \cong h_{n'}) is 8; the further reduction to cardinality profiles uses that two finite sets are isomorphic iff equinumerous. ◻

This recovers, via Yoneda, the cardinality interpretation of 5858: it is the size of any finite set whose set of self-functions, choice functions, etc., satisfy the right counting laws. “5858” is the relational profile of size-5858 finite sets within 𝑭𝒊𝒏𝑺𝒆𝒕\mathbf{FinSet}.

π\pi and ee as representing elements

The classical transcendentals admit Yoneda-flavored characterizations. We give two explicit examples.

Example 19 (The number π\pi). Let 𝑻𝒐𝒑𝑮𝒓𝒑\mathbf{TopGrp} denote the category of topological groups and continuous homomorphisms. There is a canonical continuous group homomorphism γ:(ℝ,+)→S1\gamma\colon (\mathbb{R}, +) \to S^{1}, γ(x)=eix\gamma(x) = e^{ix} (viewing S1S^{1} as the unit circle in ℂ×\mathbb{C}^{\times}), whose kernel is a discrete subgroup of ℝ\mathbb{R}. Discrete subgroups of (ℝ,+)(\mathbb{R}, +) are of the form αℤ\alpha \mathbb{Z} for α≥0\alpha \ge 0; the universal property of γ\gamma as the universal cover of S1S^{1} (a terminal object in the appropriately defined category of pointed connected covering homomorphisms onto S1S^{1}) determines this α\alpha uniquely. The number 2π2\pi is the unique positive generator of ker⁡(γ)\ker(\gamma): ker⁡(γ)=2πℤ.\ker(\gamma) = 2\pi\, \mathbb{Z}. Heuristically, π\pi is half this universal period: it labels the universal cover relation in 𝑻𝒐𝒑𝑮𝒓𝒑\mathbf{TopGrp}. By Yoneda, S1S^{1} is determined up to isomorphism by its representable functor Hom𝑻𝒐𝒑𝑮𝒓𝒑(−,S1),\mathrm{Hom}_{\mathbf{TopGrp}}(-, S^{1}), and the number 2π2\pi appears as a structural invariant within this representable, namely the generator of the kernel of the universal-cover morphism. This recovers π\pi as a relational invariant rather than as a constructed real.

Example 20 (The number ee). Consider, in the category 𝑳𝒊𝒆\mathbf{Lie} of Lie groups, the functor of continuous group homomorphisms F:𝑳𝒊𝒆→𝑺𝒆𝒕F\colon \mathbf{Lie} \to \mathbf{Set}, G↦Hom𝑳𝒊𝒆((ℝ,+),G)G \mapsto \mathrm{Hom}_{\mathbf{Lie}}((\mathbb{R}, +), G). By Yoneda this is the corepresentable h(ℝ,+)h^{(\mathbb{R}, +)}. Specializing G=(ℝ>0,⋅)G = (\mathbb{R}^{>0}, \cdot), the set F(ℝ>0)=Hom𝑳𝒊𝒆((ℝ,+),(ℝ>0,⋅))F(\mathbb{R}^{>0}) = \mathrm{Hom}_{\mathbf{Lie}}((\mathbb{R}, +), (\mathbb{R}^{>0}, \cdot)) is the one-parameter family {φa(x)=ax:a∈ℝ>0}\{\varphi_a(x) = a^x : a \in \mathbb{R}^{>0}\}. Adding the normalization φ′(0)=1\varphi'(0) = 1 cuts this down to a singleton, namely φ=exp\varphi = \exp.

The number ee is then identified by the universal-element principle (10): in the slice category of normalized continuous group homomorphisms (ℝ,+)→(ℝ>0,⋅)(\mathbb{R}, +) \to (\mathbb{R}^{>0}, \cdot) over (ℝ>0,⋅)(\mathbb{R}^{>0}, \cdot), exp\exp is the unique element, and e:=exp⁡(1)∈ℝ>0e := \exp(1) \in \mathbb{R}^{>0} is its value at the canonical generator 1∈ℝ1 \in \mathbb{R}. We acknowledge that, unlike π\pi, the example of ee is more illustrative of the Yoneda spirit (an object is the value of the unique map satisfying a normalization) than a textbook representable; the intent is to show that “ee is a relational invariant” rather than to give a fully formal Yoneda statement.

Remark 21 (The structuralist payoff). Both π\pi and ee are characterized without ever “constructing” them as Cauchy sequences or Dedekind cuts. They are positions in functor categories. The Yoneda embedding makes this position canonical: the same π\pi appears in every model.

The Density Theorem

The Yoneda embedding is fully faithful but rarely essentially surjective: most presheaves are not representable. The density theorem shows that representables nonetheless generate the presheaf category.

Theorem 22 (Density theorem). Let 𝒞\mathcal{C} be a small category. Every presheaf F:𝒞op→𝑺𝒆𝒕F\colon \mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{op}} \to \mathbf{Set} is canonically a colimit of representables. Specifically, FF is the colimit of the diagram ∫F→π𝒞→y𝒞̂,\int F \xrightarrow{\;\pi\;} \mathcal{C} \xrightarrow{\;y\;} \widehat{\mathcal{C}}, where ∫F\int F is the category of elements of FF and π\pi is the projection (X,u)↦X(X, u) \mapsto X.

Proof sketch. Define a cocone σ:yπ⇒F\sigma\colon y \pi \Rightarrow F over ∫F\int F component-wise: for (X,u)∈∫F(X, u) \in \int F, set σ(X,u):=Ψ(u):hX→F\sigma_{(X, u)} := \Psi(u)\colon h_X \to F from 5. Naturality of σ\sigma in the morphisms of ∫F\int F is exactly the Yoneda bijection. Universality: for any cocone τ:yπ⇒G\tau\colon y\pi \Rightarrow G, the unique factoring F→GF \to G assigns to u∈F(X)u \in F(X) the value τ(X,u),X(idX)∈G(X)\tau_{(X, u), X}(\mathrm{id}_X) \in G(X). ◻

Corollary 23 (Free cocompletion). The Yoneda embedding y:𝒞→𝒞̂y\colon \mathcal{C} \to \widehat{\mathcal{C}} exhibits 𝒞̂\widehat{\mathcal{C}} as the free cocompletion of 𝒞\mathcal{C}: any functor 𝒞→𝒟\mathcal{C} \to \mathcal{D} into a cocomplete category 𝒟\mathcal{D} extends uniquely (up to natural isomorphism) to a colimit-preserving functor 𝒞̂→𝒟\widehat{\mathcal{C}} \to \mathcal{D}.

Remark 24 (Numbers as colimits of probes). For the Lawvere theory ℒnat\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{nat}}, density says: any functor ℒnatop→𝑺𝒆𝒕\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{nat}}^{\mathrm{op}} \to \mathbf{Set} (an algebra over the theory) is canonically a colimit of representables hnh_n. In particular, the structure of ℕ\mathbb{N} propagates into all algebras for the theory by colimit assembly.

Beyond Numbers: Yoneda Across Mathematics

Groups as their representations

For a group GG, the regular representation is the action of GG on itself by left translation. Yoneda’s lemma in 𝑮𝒓𝒑\mathbf{Grp} specializes: Hom𝑮𝒓𝒑(ℤ,G)≅G,\mathrm{Hom}_{\mathbf{Grp}}(\mathbb{Z}, G) \cong G, identifying group elements of GG with ℤ\mathbb{Z}-points. More generally, for any small group GG regarded as a one-object groupoid 𝑩G\mathbf{B}G, the Yoneda embedding gives y:𝑩G↪𝑩Ĝ=G-𝑺𝒆𝒕,y\colon \mathbf{B}G \hookrightarrow \widehat{\mathbf{B}G} = G\text{-}\mathbf{Set}, sending the unique object to the regular GG-set GG. The Yoneda lemma then yields Nat(G,F)≅F(*)(the underlying set of F),\mathrm{Nat}(G, F) \cong F(*) \quad \text{(the underlying set of $F$),} i.e., a GG-equivariant map out of the regular representation is determined by where it sends the identity. This is Cayley’s theorem in disguise.

Proposition 25 (Cayley via Yoneda). The Yoneda embedding y:𝑩G↪G-𝑺𝒆𝒕y\colon \mathbf{B}G \hookrightarrow G\text{-}\mathbf{Set} realizes Cayley’s theorem: every group is canonically a subgroup of the symmetric group on its underlying set, via the regular representation.

Proof. Fully faithfulness of yy implies Aut(G as G-set)=G\mathrm{Aut}(G \text{ as $G$-set}) = G, embedding G↪Sym(G)G \hookrightarrow \mathrm{Sym}(G). ◻

Spaces via points

For a topological space XX, the singular complex S(X):=Hom𝑻𝒐𝒑(Δ•,X)S(X) := \mathrm{Hom}_{\mathbf{Top}}(\Delta^{\bullet}, X) is a simplicial set. This is the covariant Yoneda restricted to the cosimplicial space of standard simplices: X∈𝑻𝒐𝒑X \in \mathbf{Top} is probed by morphisms from the Δn\Delta^n. The Quillen equivalence between simplicial sets and topological spaces, restricted to weak equivalences, recovers XX up to homotopy from S(X)S(X) — a homotopical Yoneda.

Schemes as functors of points

Grothendieck’s revolution in algebraic geometry consists of taking the Yoneda embedding seriously.

Definition 26 (Functor of points). Given a scheme XX, its functor of points is hX:𝑺𝒄𝒉op→𝑺𝒆𝒕,T↦Hom𝑺𝒄𝒉(T,X).h_X\colon \mathbf{Sch}^{\mathrm{op}} \longrightarrow \mathbf{Set},\qquad T \longmapsto \mathrm{Hom}_{\mathbf{Sch}}(T, X). Equivalently, restricting to affine test schemes T=Spec(R)T = \mathrm{Spec}(R), one obtains a functor 𝑹𝒊𝒏𝒈→𝑺𝒆𝒕\mathbf{Ring} \to \mathbf{Set}, R↦X(R)R \mapsto X(R), the set of RR-points of XX.

By the Yoneda lemma, XX is determined up to canonical isomorphism by hXh_X. This is the basis for treating schemes as functors 𝑹𝒊𝒏𝒈→𝑺𝒆𝒕\mathbf{Ring} \to \mathbf{Set} satisfying suitable sheaf conditions: representable functors. Moduli problems are typically posed by writing down a non-representable presheaf (the moduli functor) and asking whether a representing scheme (or stack) exists.

Example 27 (𝔸ℤ1\mathbb{A}^1_{\mathbb{Z}} as a representable functor). The affine line 𝔸ℤ1\mathbb{A}^{1}_{\mathbb{Z}} has functor of points R↦RR \mapsto R, the forgetful functor 𝑹𝒊𝒏𝒈→𝑺𝒆𝒕\mathbf{Ring} \to \mathbf{Set}. By Yoneda, 𝔸1\mathbb{A}^{1} is uniquely determined as the scheme representing this functor.

Enriched, 22-Categorical, and ∞\infty-Categorical Yoneda

Enriched Yoneda

Definition 28 (𝒱\mathcal{V}-enriched category). Let 𝒱=(𝒱0,⊗,I)\mathcal{V} = (\mathcal{V}_0, \otimes, I) be a closed monoidal category. A 𝒱\mathcal{V}-category 𝒞\mathcal{C} has a class of objects and, for each pair X,YX, Y, a hom-object 𝒞(X,Y)∈𝒱\mathcal{C}(X, Y) \in \mathcal{V}, with composition 𝒞(Y,Z)⊗𝒞(X,Y)→𝒞(X,Z)\mathcal{C}(Y, Z) \otimes \mathcal{C}(X, Y) \to \mathcal{C}(X, Z) and identities I→𝒞(X,X)I \to \mathcal{C}(X, X) satisfying associativity and unit axioms.

Theorem 29 (Enriched Yoneda lemma). For a 𝒱\mathcal{V}-category 𝒞\mathcal{C}, 𝒱\mathcal{V}-functor F:𝒞op→𝒱F\colon \mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{op}} \to \mathcal{V}, and X∈𝒞X \in \mathcal{C}, there is a 𝒱\mathcal{V}-natural isomorphism [𝒞op,𝒱](𝒞(−,X),F)≅F(X)in 𝒱.[\mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{op}}, \mathcal{V}](\mathcal{C}(-, X), F) \cong F(X) \quad \text{in $\mathcal{V}$.} The enriched Yoneda embedding y:𝒞→[𝒞op,𝒱]y\colon \mathcal{C} \to [\mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{op}}, \mathcal{V}] is fully faithful as a 𝒱\mathcal{V}-functor.

Examples:

  • 𝒱=𝑺𝒆𝒕\mathcal{V} = \mathbf{Set}: ordinary Yoneda.

  • 𝒱=𝑪𝒂𝒕\mathcal{V} = \mathbf{Cat}: 22-Yoneda (next subsection).

  • 𝒱=𝑨𝒃\mathcal{V} = \mathbf{Ab}: Yoneda for additive categories; representable functors are abelian groups.

  • 𝒱=𝑽𝒆𝒄𝒕k\mathcal{V} = \mathbf{Vect}_k: Yoneda for kk-linear categories; representables are vector spaces.

  • 𝒱=𝒔𝑺𝒆𝒕\mathcal{V} = \mathbf{sSet}: simplicial Yoneda, the basis for ∞\infty-Yoneda.

22-categorical Yoneda

In a 22-category 𝒦\mathcal{K}, the hom is a category 𝒦(X,Y)\mathcal{K}(X, Y) with morphisms (1-cells) and morphisms-between-morphisms (2-cells).

Theorem 30 (22-Yoneda). For a 22-category 𝒦\mathcal{K}, the 22-Yoneda embedding y:𝒦→[𝒦op,𝑪𝒂𝒕]y\colon \mathcal{K} \to [\mathcal{K}^{\mathrm{op}}, \mathbf{Cat}] is fully faithful at the level of 11-cells and 22-cells. Concretely, [𝒦op,𝑪𝒂𝒕](𝒦(−,X),F)≃F(X)[\mathcal{K}^{\mathrm{op}}, \mathbf{Cat}](\mathcal{K}(-, X), F) \simeq F(X) as categories, where ≃\simeq denotes equivalence.

∞\infty-categorical Yoneda

For an (∞,1)(\infty, 1)-category 𝒞\mathcal{C} presented as a quasi-category (i.e., a simplicial set satisfying the inner horn lifting condition), the Yoneda embedding lifts to y:𝒞↪𝒫(𝒞):=Fun(𝒞op,𝒮),y\colon \mathcal{C} \hookrightarrow \mathcal{P}(\mathcal{C}) := \mathrm{Fun}(\mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{op}}, \mathcal{S}), where 𝒮\mathcal{S} is the ∞\infty-category of spaces (Kan complexes).

Theorem 31 (∞\infty-Yoneda lemma, Lurie). For a small (∞,1)(\infty, 1)-category 𝒞\mathcal{C}, an object X∈𝒞X \in \mathcal{C}, and a presheaf F∈𝒫(𝒞)F \in \mathcal{P}(\mathcal{C}), there is an equivalence of spaces Map𝒫(𝒞)(𝒞(−,X),F)≃F(X).\mathrm{Map}_{\mathcal{P}(\mathcal{C})}(\mathcal{C}(-, X), F) \simeq F(X). The Yoneda embedding is fully faithful in the ∞\infty-categorical sense, and exhibits 𝒫(𝒞)\mathcal{P}(\mathcal{C}) as the free cocompletion of 𝒞\mathcal{C} under small homotopy colimits.

The reference is Lurie’s Higher Topos Theory , Lemma 5.1.5.2 and Theorem 5.1.5.6.

Ramifications

The progression Set-Yoneda →\to enriched-Yoneda →\to ∞\infty-Yoneda parallels the progression of structuralist principles:

  • Set-Yoneda: an object is its hom-set profile.

  • Enriched-Yoneda: an object is its hom-object profile in 𝒱\mathcal{V}.

  • ∞\infty-Yoneda: an object is its mapping-space profile, with all higher coherences.

Each generalization preserves the slogan “an object is its system of relationships,” but enlarges the meaning of “relationship.”

Bridge to Homotopy Type Theory

Yoneda yields equivalences; univalence yields equalities

8 states X≅Y⇔hX≅hYX \cong Y \iff h_X \cong h_Y. The right-hand isomorphism is a piece of data: a natural transformation in each direction whose composites are identities. In ordinary category theory, this is as far as we can push: X≅YX \cong Y is not the same as X=YX = Y; the latter requires a notion of identity stronger than isomorphism.

Definition 32 (Univalence axiom, informal). In Homotopy Type Theory, for any types A,BA, B in a universe 𝒰\mathcal{U}, the canonical map idtoeqv:(A=B)→(A≃B)\mathrm{idtoeqv}\colon (A = B) \longrightarrow (A \simeq B) is itself an equivalence.

The univalence axiom internalizes Yoneda’s identity criterion. Where the Yoneda lemma states hX≅hY⇒X≅Yh_X \cong h_Y \Rightarrow X \cong Y, univalence states hX≃hY⇒hX=hYh_X \simeq h_Y \Rightarrow h_X = h_Y as inhabitants of the universe of presheaves. The equivalence becomes a path.

The directed analogue

The Yoneda embedding has a directed (non-symmetric) analogue in directed type theory and simplicial type theory . There, the role of mapping spaces is played by directed hom-types hom⁡A(x,y)\hom_A(x, y), and the Yoneda lemma takes the form Map(hom⁡A(−,x),F)≃F(x),\mathrm{Map}(\hom_A(-, x), F) \simeq F(x), where “Map” itself is a directed function space. This is the entry point for an internal language of (∞,1)(\infty, 1)-categories. We mention it for context; its development belongs to a sequel.

Sketch of the embedding code

Pseudocode for the central operation. The presheaf hX=Hom(−,X)h_X = \mathrm{Hom}(-, X) is contravariant in its argument: for a morphism g:W→Zg\colon W \to Z in 𝒞\mathcal{C}, hX(g):Hom(Z,X)→Hom(W,X)h_X(g)\colon \mathrm{Hom}(Z, X) \to \mathrm{Hom}(W, X) is precomposition by gg, sending m:Z→Xm\colon Z \to X to m∘g:W→Xm \circ g\colon W \to X. Thus:

yonedaAt :: Category c => c -> Obj c -> (Obj c -> [Mor c])
yonedaAt cat x = \z -> hom cat z x

-- representable presheaf h_x = Hom(-, x).
-- onMor takes a morphism g : w -> z in the base category C
-- and an element m : z -> x of h_x(z), and returns m . g : w -> x
-- (precomposition by g, since presheaves are contravariant).
representable :: Category c => c -> Obj c -> Presheaf c
representable cat x =
  Presheaf
    { onObj = \z -> hom cat z x
    , onMor = \(g :: Mor c) m -> compose cat m g  -- m : z -> x, g : w -> z
    }

The onMor component of a presheaf must produce a function F(Z)→F(W)F(Z) \to F(W) from a morphism g:W→Zg\colon W \to Z. For the representable presheaf hX=Hom(−,X)h_X = \mathrm{Hom}(-, X), this map is precomposition with gg: it sends a morphism m:Z→Xm\colon Z \to X to the composite m∘g:W→Xm \circ g\colon W \to X. The full code includes the natural-transformation type, a Yoneda-lemma-witness function constructing the bijection Φ\Phi explicitly, and an eq function that checks equality of two finite presheaves up to isomorphism.

Discussion

Comparison with Paper III

Paper III (Universal Property) characterizes individual objects (the natural number object, the real numbers, π\pi, ee) by their respective universal properties. Each such characterization, viewed through Yoneda, is the assertion that some presheaf is representable. Paper III’s content is recovered by listing one universal element per topic; Paper IV recovers it as a single principle (10).

Limitations

The Yoneda lemma assumes locally small: for not-locally-small categories (e.g., 𝑪𝒂𝒕\mathbf{Cat} enlarged), the embedding lands in a category of large presheaves and care is needed with size hierarchies. The ∞\infty-Yoneda lemma further requires an explicit model (quasi-categories, complete Segal spaces) and the Lurie–Joyal machinery.

The interpretation “5858 is h58h_{58}” is sensitive to the ambient category. The structuralist achievement

Mathematical structuralism, as articulated by Benacerraf, Resnik, and Shapiro, holds that mathematical objects are positions in structures rather than entities with intrinsic identity. The Yoneda lemma is, arguably, the strongest form of this thesis: an object literally is its relational profile, recoverable up to isomorphism from hXh_X. 8 renders structuralism a theorem rather than a philosophical stance.

The remaining gap — between “isomorphic” and “identical” — is what HoTT closes. 8 sketched the bridge; Paper V develops it. Connections to representation theory and physics

The Yoneda perspective unifies several apparent dualities in mathematics. In representation theory, an abstract group GG is determined by its category of representations (Tannakian duality). In algebraic geometry, a scheme XX is determined by its functor of points hXh_X (Grothendieck). In algebraic topology, a space XX up to homotopy is determined by its mapping spaces Map(K,X)\mathrm{Map}(K, X) as KK ranges over CW complexes (Brown representability).

Each of these is, at root, an instance of the Yoneda principle: the object is the functor it represents, and structural inquiries reduce to functorial ones. The same pattern recurs in physics: a quantum field theory is, in the Atiyah–Segal axiomatization, a functor Bordn→𝑽𝒆𝒄𝒕\mathrm{Bord}_n \to \mathbf{Vect}, and physical observables are exactly the Yoneda data of this functor. The hypothesis that “mathematics is the science of structure” is, post-Yoneda, the hypothesis that mathematics is the science of representable functors.

Pedagogical remarks

For a student first encountering the Yoneda lemma, the following pedagogical sequence has proved effective. (i) Begin with the order category (ℕ,≤)(\mathbb{N}, \le) to see hnh_n as a profile of singletons and zeros. (ii) Move to Cayley’s theorem in 𝑩G\mathbf{B}G to feel the regular representation. (iii) Reach Lawvere’s theory ℒnat\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{nat}} to see how counting laws emerge from |hn(m)|=nm|h_n(m)| = n^m. (iv) Generalize to functors of points for schemes, where X(R)=hX(Spec(R))X(R) = h_X(\mathrm{Spec}(R)) becomes a familiar construction. (v) Only then state the lemma in full generality. The Haskell implementation accompanying this paper supports each of (i)–(iii) directly.

Conclusion

The Yoneda embedding y:𝒞↪[𝒞op,𝑺𝒆𝒕]y\colon \mathcal{C} \hookrightarrow [\mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{op}}, \mathbf{Set}] is the structuralist’s theorem made formal. The natural number 5858 is the representable functor Hom(−,58)\mathrm{Hom}(-, 58); what remains, under change of category, are particular presentations of the same relational profile. Beyond arithmetic, the same principle structures groups (Cayley), spaces (singular complex), and schemes (functor of points). Enriched, 22-categorical, and ∞\infty-categorical generalizations show the principle survives every increase in mathematical sophistication.

The Yoneda lemma alone does not collapse the distinction between isomorphism and identity; it produces the former from the latter. Univalence (Paper V) closes the gap by axiomatizing the Yoneda corollary as a path in the universe. Together, these two principles — one categorical, one type-theoretical — form the technical core of the Univalent Correspondence.
