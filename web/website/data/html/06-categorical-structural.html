Abstract

We develop the structural / categorical answer to the question “what is a number?” by treating numerals as invariants under all structure-preserving morphisms between models of arithmetic. We survey mathematical structuralism in the philosophical tradition (Resnik, Shapiro, Hellman, Awodey), present its categorical formalization through Lawvere’s Elementary Theory of the Category of Sets (ETCS) and functorial semantics of algebraic theories, and prove an isomorphism-invariance theorem for first-order properties of finitely presented theories. We define a small algebraic theory of pointed unary structures, exhibit two non-equal but isomorphic set-theoretic models, and verify — both formally and by accompanying executable Haskell code — that all “numerical answers” (cardinalities of definable subsets, values of definable functions) coincide. We connect categorification (a 5858-element groupoid in 𝑮𝒑𝒅\mathbf{Gpd} that decategorifies to 5858 in 𝑺𝒆𝒕\mathbf{Set} via π0\pi_0) and Euler-characteristic / KK-theoretic decategorification to the same invariance principle, and we end at the bridge to the synthesis paper: the Yoneda embedding, universal properties, and Voevodsky’s univalence axiom are three views of one phenomenon, namely that structural identity is identity. Univalence makes the meta-condition “a property is structural iff it is preserved under isomorphism” an internal theorem rather than an external rule of practice.

Introduction

The structural turn

Frege asked what numbers are; Dedekind answered: numbers are positions in a structure satisfying the simply infinite system axioms . Frege rejected the answer because it left the structure underdetermined —“many systems”, not one. The twentieth century proved Dedekind right and Frege wrong: every system satisfying the axioms is canonically isomorphic to every other, and the only invariants that survive a change of model are the structural ones. Benacerraf  sharpened the point: since infinitely many set-theoretic encodings (von Neumann’s, Zermelo’s, and uncountably many others) all satisfy Peano’s axioms, no encoding can claim to be the natural numbers without contradiction. Numbers, if they are anything, must be what is preserved under all such encodings.

This paper formalizes that thesis. We argue that:

  1. “5858” denotes an invariant under all structure-preserving morphisms between models of arithmetic, in a sense made precise by functorial semantics (4).

  2. Mathematical structuralism — in its ante rem, in re, and modal variants — is the philosophical commitment that this is the right level of analysis.

  3. Category theory (and ETCS in particular) is the natural formal language because it is built around the principle that objects are determined up to isomorphism by their relationships.

  4. Homotopy Type Theory (HoTT) with the univalence axiom turns “up to isomorphism” from an external constraint into an internal theorem.

The paper is the last of the six topical papers in the Univalent Correspondence Project and provides the bridge to the subsequent synthesis paper. The thesis we defend across all six papers is captured by the columns table reproduced (in shortened form) in 1.

Mathematical Framework

We fix terminology and notation. All categories are locally small unless stated otherwise; standard references for the categorical background are Mac Lane  and Riehl .

Definition 1 (Category). A category 𝒞\mathcal{C} consists of a class Ob(𝒞)\mathrm{Ob}(\mathcal{C}) of objects, for each pair A,B∈Ob(𝒞)A,B \in \mathrm{Ob}(\mathcal{C}) a set Hom𝒞(A,B)\mathrm{Hom}_\mathcal{C}(A,B) of morphisms, an associative composition, and identity morphisms.

Definition 2 (Functor). A functor F:𝒞→𝒟F : \mathcal{C}\to \mathcal{D} assigns to each A∈Ob(𝒞)A \in \mathrm{Ob}(\mathcal{C}) an object F(A)∈Ob(𝒟)F(A) \in \mathrm{Ob}(\mathcal{D}) and to each f:A→Bf : A \to B a morphism F(f):F(A)→F(B)F(f) : F(A) \to F(B), preserving composition and identities.

Definition 3 (Natural transformation). Given functors F,G:𝒞→𝒟F, G : \mathcal{C}\to \mathcal{D}, a natural transformation α:F⇒G\alpha : F \Rightarrow G is a family {αA:F(A)→G(A)}A∈Ob(𝒞)\{\alpha_A : F(A) \to G(A)\}_{A \in \mathrm{Ob}(\mathcal{C})} such that for every f:A→Bf : A \to B, G(f)∘αA=αB∘F(f)G(f) \circ \alpha_A = \alpha_B \circ F(f).

Definition 4 (Isomorphism). A morphism f:A→Bf : A \to B in 𝒞\mathcal{C} is an isomorphism if there exists g:B→Ag : B \to A with g∘f=idAg \circ f = \mathrm{id}_A and f∘g=idBf \circ g = \mathrm{id}_B. We write A≅BA \cong B when an isomorphism exists.

Definition 5 (Equivalence of categories). Categories 𝒞\mathcal{C} and 𝒟\mathcal{D} are equivalent, written 𝒞≃𝒟\mathcal{C}\simeq\mathcal{D}, if there exist functors F:𝒞→𝒟F : \mathcal{C}\to \mathcal{D}, G:𝒟→𝒞G : \mathcal{D}\to \mathcal{C} with natural isomorphisms GF≅id𝒞G F \cong\mathrm{id}_\mathcal{C} and FG≅id𝒟F G \cong\mathrm{id}_\mathcal{D}.

Definition 6 (Algebraic theory, in the Lawvere sense). An algebraic theory (in the sense of Lawvere ) is a small category 𝒯\mathcal{T} with finite products, equipped with a distinguished object X∈Ob(𝒯)X \in \mathrm{Ob}(\mathcal{T}) such that every object is a finite power XnX^n for some n≥0n \geq 0.

Definition 7 (Model / interpretation). A model of 𝒯\mathcal{T} in a category ℰ\mathcal{E} with finite products is a finite-product-preserving functor M:𝒯→ℰM : \mathcal{T}\to \mathcal{E}. The category Mod⁡(𝒯)≔FP(𝒯,ℰ)\operatorname{Mod}(\mathcal{T}) \coloneqq\mathrm{FP}(\mathcal{T}, \mathcal{E}) has finite-product-preserving functors as objects and natural transformations between them as morphisms.

Remark 8 (Models in 𝑺𝒆𝒕\mathbf{Set}). When ℰ=𝑺𝒆𝒕\mathcal{E}= \mathbf{Set}, an interpretation M:𝒯→𝑺𝒆𝒕M : \mathcal{T}\to \mathbf{Set} assigns to the generating object XX a set M(X)M(X) called the carrier. The other generating data of 𝒯\mathcal{T} (operations, equations) are interpreted as functions and equalities on M(X)M(X). This is the standard universal-algebraic notion of model.

Definition 9 (Structure-preserving morphism). A morphism of 𝒯\mathcal{T}-models from MM to M′M' is a natural transformation α:M⇒M′\alpha : M \Rightarrow M'. By naturality and finite-product preservation, α\alpha is determined by its component αX:M(X)→M′(X)\alpha_X : M(X) \to M'(X), which is a homomorphism in the universal-algebraic sense.

Mathematical Structuralism

The thesis

Mathematical structuralism (Benacerraf , Resnik , Shapiro , Hellman , Awodey ) is the view that mathematics studies structures rather than objects: the natural numbers are not a particular set or sequence but the abstract pattern that all ℕ\mathbb{N}-like structures share.

Resnik’s slogan  is that mathematical objects are “positions in patterns”. Shapiro distinguishes the position itself (an abstract place) from the objects that may occupy it. Hellman  formulates the doctrine modally: a structure is a possible-world description, and arithmetic asserts what would be true of any system that realizes the structure.

Three flavors

Definition 10 (Ante rem structuralism, Shapiro). The structures of mathematics exist independently of any particular system that realizes them. Numbers are real abstract entities, but their identity is exhausted by their relational properties.

Definition 11 (In re structuralism, Resnik). Mathematical structures are abstractions from collections of similar systems. There is no structure “in itself”; only patterns instantiated by concrete or abstract systems.

Definition 12 (Modal / eliminative structuralism, Hellman). There are no abstract structures or numbers; statements of arithmetic are read as universally quantified conditionals over all possible ℕ\mathbb{N}-like systems: “In any ω\omega-sequence, the seventh element after zero satisfies …\dots” rather than “77 satisfies …\dots”.

Example 13 (Three views of 5858).

  • Ante rem: 5858 is an abstract position in the unique structure ⟨ℕ,0,s⟩\langle \mathbb{N}, 0, \mathrm{s}\rangle.

  • In re: 5858 is whatever is common to all things obtained by applying s\mathrm{s} exactly 5858 times to 00 in any concrete realization.

  • Modal: “58+1=5958 + 1 = 59” means “it is necessary that, in any ω\omega-sequence, the 5858th iterate’s successor is the 5959th iterate”.

Awodey’s category-theoretic structuralism

Awodey  argues that category theory is structuralism realized: “mathematical objects are determined up to isomorphism by their relations to other objects in the appropriate category, and structural properties are exactly those preserved by isomorphisms.” This is not a metaphysical claim but a methodological one: working categorically is working structurally.

Proposition 14 (Structural properties are isomorphism-invariant). Let 𝒞\mathcal{C} be a category and let PP be a property of objects of 𝒞\mathcal{C} that is expressible using only the data of 𝒞\mathcal{C} (objects, morphisms, composition, and finite limits/colimits where they exist). Then for any isomorphism f:A→Bf : A \to B, P(A)⇔P(B)P(A) \iff P(B).

Proof sketch. By induction on the complexity of PP. Atomic categorical statements (commutativity of squares, existence of fillers, equality of composites) are preserved by isomorphisms because ff and f−1f^{-1} are inverse, and composition is preserved. For complex PP, the inductive step is straightforward. ◻

Remark 15. 14 is meta-mathematical: it presupposes a notion of “categorical formula” and identifies a syntactic class of properties whose semantics is invariant under isomorphism. In HoTT this becomes an internal theorem, the structure identity principle (50).

Comparison and reconciliation

The three flavors of structuralism are not in pairwise contradiction so much as in pairwise emphasis. We may organize them as follows.

The categorical / structural perspective is compatible with each of the philosophical readings. Reading “Mod⁡(Ts)\operatorname{Mod}(T_\mathrm{s})” as a Platonic abstract category gives the ante-rem version; reading it as a class of concrete realizations gives the in-re version; reading it as a possible-world quantifier gives the modal version. The functorial-semantics framework of 4 is metaphysically neutral; what it secures is the invariance principle that all three philosophical readings agree must hold.

Remark 16 (Awodey on univalence). Awodey’s later work  extends this neutrality to univalent foundations: “the univalence axiom is the principle that makes the structuralist viewpoint formally consistent inside a foundational system”. We return to this in 9.

Functorial Semantics

Lawvere theories

We follow Lawvere’s 1963 Ph.D. thesis : an algebraic theory is a category, models are functors, and homomorphisms are natural transformations. This rephrasing is consequential: we obtain a precise sense in which two models “compute the same number”.

Definition 17 (Lawvere theory). A Lawvere theory is a small category 𝒯\mathcal{T} with finite products, equipped with a generator XX such that every object of 𝒯\mathcal{T} is (canonically) a finite power XnX^n, n≥0n \geq 0. Equivalently, 𝒯op\mathcal{T}^\mathrm{op} has finite coproducts and is the opposite of the free finite-product category on a single object plus operation symbols and axioms.

Example 18 (The theory TsT_\mathrm{s} of pointed unary structures). Let TsT_\mathrm{s} be the Lawvere theory generated by:

  • one object XX,

  • a constant z:X0→X\mathrm{z}: X^0 \to X (the “zero”),

  • a unary operation s:X→X\mathrm{s}: X \to X (the “successor”),

with no equations. A model in 𝑺𝒆𝒕\mathbf{Set} is a triple (M,z,s)(M, z, s) where MM is a set, z∈Mz \in M, and s:M→Ms : M \to M.

Definition 19 (Initial successor structure). A model (N,0,s)∈Mod⁡(Ts)(N, 0, \mathrm{s}) \in \operatorname{Mod}(T_\mathrm{s}) is initial if for every (M,z,s)(M, z, s) there is a unique TsT_\mathrm{s}-homomorphism h:N→Mh : N \to M with h(0)=zh(0) = z and h∘s=s∘hh \circ \mathrm{s}= s \circ h.

Theorem 20 (Standard model of arithmetic). (ℕ,0,s)(\mathbb{N}, 0, \mathrm{s}) with s(n)=n+1\mathrm{s}(n) = n+1 is initial in Mod⁡(Ts)\operatorname{Mod}(T_\mathrm{s}), and any two initial models are uniquely isomorphic.

Proof. Existence and uniqueness of hh are by primitive recursion. Two initial objects are connected by a unique morphism each way; their composites are identity by uniqueness. ◻

Category of models

Definition 21. For a Lawvere theory 𝒯\mathcal{T}, let Mod⁡(𝒯)\operatorname{Mod}(\mathcal{T}) denote the category of finite-product-preserving functors 𝒯→𝑺𝒆𝒕\mathcal{T}\to \mathbf{Set} with natural transformations as morphisms.

Proposition 22. Let 𝒯=Ts\mathcal{T}= T_\mathrm{s} and let M,M′:𝒯→𝑺𝒆𝒕M, M' : \mathcal{T}\to \mathbf{Set}. The data of a natural transformation α:M⇒M′\alpha : M \Rightarrow M' is exactly a function αX:M(X)→M′(X)\alpha_X : M(X) \to M'(X) such that αX(M(z)(*))=M′(z)(*),αX∘M(s)=M′(s)∘αX.\alpha_X(M(\mathrm{z})(\ast)) = M'(\mathrm{z})(\ast), \quad \alpha_X \circ M(\mathrm{s}) = M'(\mathrm{s}) \circ \alpha_X.

Proof. Naturality of α\alpha on the morphism z:X0→X\mathrm{z}: X^0 \to X asserts the commutativity of $$\begin{tikzcd} M(X^0) \arrow[r, "M(\mathrm{z})"] \arrow[d, "\alpha_{X^0}"'] & M(X) \arrow[d, "\alpha_X"] \\ M'(X^0) \arrow[r, "M'(\mathrm{z})"'] & M'(X) \end{tikzcd}$$ Since both functors preserve finite products, M(X0)M(X^0) and M′(X0)M'(X^0) are terminal in 𝑺𝒆𝒕\mathbf{Set} (singletons {*}\{\ast\}) and αX0\alpha_{X^0} is the unique map between them; the diagram thus collapses to the first displayed equation. Naturality on s:X→X\mathrm{s}: X \to X gives directly the second displayed equation. Conversely, any pair (αX0,αX)(\alpha_{X^0}, \alpha_X) satisfying these two equations extends uniquely to a natural transformation: finite-product preservation determines all higher components αXn\alpha_{X^n} as the nn-fold product of αX\alpha_X with itself. ◻

Definable invariants

We now make precise the notion of “numerical answer extracted from a model”.

Definition 23 (Definable invariant). Let 𝒯\mathcal{T} be a Lawvere theory and ℰ\mathcal{E} a category. A definable invariant of 𝒯\mathcal{T}-models in ℰ\mathcal{E} is a function I:Ob(Mod⁡(𝒯))→Ob(ℰ)I : \mathrm{Ob}(\operatorname{Mod}(\mathcal{T})) \to \mathrm{Ob}(\mathcal{E}) together with, for every isomorphism α:M→M′\alpha : M \to M' in Mod⁡(𝒯)\operatorname{Mod}(\mathcal{T}), an isomorphism I(α):I(M)→I(M′)I(\alpha) : I(M) \to I(M') in ℰ\mathcal{E}, such that I(idM)=idI(M)I(\mathrm{id}_M) = \mathrm{id}_{I(M)} and II preserves composition. Equivalently, II is a functor from the underlying groupoid of Mod⁡(𝒯)\operatorname{Mod}(\mathcal{T}) to ℰ\mathcal{E}.

Example 24. Cardinality is a definable invariant when restricted to finite models: |M|=|M′||M| = |M'| whenever M≅M′M \cong M'. This is the simplest example of the structural-invariance phenomenon.

Example 25 (Order of an element). For TsT_\mathrm{s}-models (M,z,s)(M,z,s) in 𝑺𝒆𝒕\mathbf{Set}, define ord(M,z,s)=min⁡{n≥1:sn(z)=z}\mathrm{ord}(M,z,s) = \min \{ n \geq 1 : s^n(z) = z \} if such nn exists, ∞\infty otherwise. This is a definable invariant: any isomorphism preserves the orbit of zz under ss.

Theorem 26 (Invariance theorem). Let 𝒯\mathcal{T} be a Lawvere theory and II a definable invariant. For any two isomorphic models M,M′∈Mod⁡(𝒯)M, M' \in \operatorname{Mod}(\mathcal{T}), I(M)≅I(M′)I(M) \cong I(M') in ℰ\mathcal{E}. In particular, when ℰ=𝑺𝒆𝒕\mathcal{E}= \mathbf{Set} and I(M)I(M) is a (finite) cardinal, |I(M)|=|I(M′)||I(M)| = |I(M')|.

Proof. By 23, II acts functorially on the groupoid of isomorphisms. Functors send isomorphisms to isomorphisms, so I(M)≅I(M′)I(M) \cong I(M'). Cardinality is itself an isomorphism invariant on 𝑺𝒆𝒕\mathbf{Set}, so |I(M)|=|I(M′)||I(M)| = |I(M')|. ◻

Corollary 27 (Structural identity of models). If M≅M′M \cong M' in Mod⁡(𝒯)\operatorname{Mod}(\mathcal{T}), then MM and M′M' agree on every definable invariant. In particular, every “numerical answer” computed from MM matches the corresponding answer computed from M′M'.

Two Models, One Answer

We make 26 concrete with two non-equal but isomorphic models of TsT_\mathrm{s}. The accompanying Haskell code (11) verifies each computed invariant.

Example 28 (von Neumann vs. Zermelo). Define two finite TsT_\mathrm{s}-models: MvN(X)={∅,{∅},{∅,{∅}},{∅,{∅},{∅,{∅}}}},MZ(X)={∅,{∅},{{∅}},{{{∅}}}},\begin{align*} M_{\mathrm{vN}}(X) &= \{\emptyset, \{\emptyset\}, \{\emptyset,\{\emptyset\}\}, \{\emptyset,\{\emptyset\},\{\emptyset,\{\emptyset\}\}\}\}, \\ M_{\mathrm{Z}}(X) &= \{\emptyset, \{\emptyset\}, \{\{\emptyset\}\}, \{\{\{\emptyset\}\}\}\}, \end{align*} each with z\mathrm{z} interpreted as ∅\emptyset and s\mathrm{s} interpreted by the appropriate generation rule (von Neumann: n↦n∪{n}n \mapsto n \cup \{n\}; Zermelo: n↦{n}n \mapsto \{n\}).

Proposition 29. MvN≅MZM_{\mathrm{vN}} \cong M_{\mathrm{Z}} in Mod⁡(Ts)\operatorname{Mod}(T_\mathrm{s}), but MvN(X)≠MZ(X)M_{\mathrm{vN}}(X) \neq M_{\mathrm{Z}}(X) as sets in ZFC.

Proof. Define αX(MvN(sk)(∅))=MZ(sk)(∅)\alpha_X(M_{\mathrm{vN}}(\mathrm{s}^k)(\emptyset)) = M_{\mathrm{Z}}(\mathrm{s}^k)(\emptyset) for k=0,1,2,3k = 0, 1, 2, 3. By construction αX(∅)=∅\alpha_X(\emptyset) = \emptyset and αX∘MvN(s)=MZ(s)∘αX\alpha_X \circ M_{\mathrm{vN}}(\mathrm{s}) = M_{\mathrm{Z}}(\mathrm{s}) \circ \alpha_X, so α\alpha is a natural transformation by 22. It is bijective on carriers, hence an isomorphism. Set-theoretic non-equality follows from {∅,{∅}}∈MvN(X)\{\emptyset,\{\emptyset\}\} \in M_{\mathrm{vN}}(X) but {∅,{∅}}∉MZ(X)\{\emptyset,\{\emptyset\}\} \notin M_{\mathrm{Z}}(X) (and vice versa for {{{∅}}}\{\{\{\emptyset\}\}\}). ◻

Remark 30 (The Benacerraf situation). 29 is a finite, fully verifiable instance of Benacerraf’s identification problem : two structurally identical models that disagree on every set-membership question (“Is {∅}∈2\{\emptyset\} \in 2?” is true in MvNM_{\mathrm{vN}}, false in MZM_{\mathrm{Z}}). The structuralist conclusion: such questions are not arithmetic; they are encoding-relative.

Definable answers agree

Theorem 31 (Agreement of numerical invariants). For every definable invariant II of TsT_\mathrm{s}-models in 𝑺𝒆𝒕\mathbf{Set} (or in ℕ\mathbb{N} via cardinality), I(MvN)=I(MZ)I(M_{\mathrm{vN}}) = I(M_{\mathrm{Z}}).

Proof. By 29, MvN≅MZM_{\mathrm{vN}} \cong M_{\mathrm{Z}}. By 26, I(MvN)≅I(MZ)I(M_{\mathrm{vN}}) \cong I(M_{\mathrm{Z}}). For invariants taking values in ℕ\mathbb{N} (cardinalities, orbit lengths, fixed-point counts), an isomorphism in ℕ\mathbb{N} is equality. ◻

The Haskell implementation in 11 performs this verification computationally: it builds both models, an isomorphism between them, and a panel of definable invariants, and checks term-by-term that the computed values agree.

Morphisms of Models in Algebra

The framework of 4 extends to richer algebraic theories. We collect three illustrative cases.

Ring homomorphisms

The Lawvere theory TringT_{\mathrm{ring}} has signature {0,1,+,−,⋅}\{0, 1, +, -, \cdot\} and the standard ring axioms. A morphism of TringT_{\mathrm{ring}}-models is a ring homomorphism: a function f:R→R′f : R \to R' with f(0)=0,f(1)=1,f(x+y)=f(x)+f(y),f(x⋅y)=f(x)⋅f(y).f(0) = 0, \quad f(1) = 1, \quad f(x + y) = f(x) + f(y), \quad f(x \cdot y) = f(x) \cdot f(y). The structural invariants of a ring (commutativity, characteristic, dimension as a ℤ\mathbb{Z}-module, prime spectrum, KK-theory) are preserved by isomorphism. Specifically, ℤ\mathbb{Z} is initial in Mod⁡(Tring)\operatorname{Mod}(T_{\mathrm{ring}}): there is a unique ring homomorphism ℤ→R\mathbb{Z}\to R for any ring RR, and it is the structural identification of integers inside RR.

Example 32 (Integers in a finite ring). Let R=ℤ/12ℤR = \mathbb{Z}/12\mathbb{Z}. The unique morphism ι:ℤ→R\iota : \mathbb{Z}\to R is the canonical projection. The image ι(58)=58mod⁡12=10\iota(58) = 58 \bmod 12 = 10 is the structural representative of “5858” in RR. Different rings give different representatives; the structural invariant is the function R↦ιR(58)R \mapsto \iota_R(58), which is a natural transformation ι(−)(58):1⇒U\iota(-)(58) : 1 \Rightarrow U (where U:𝑹𝒊𝒏𝒈→𝑺𝒆𝒕U : \mathbf{Ring}\to \mathbf{Set} is the underlying-set functor).

Ordered field embeddings

The theory of ordered fields has signature {0,1,+,−,⋅,−1,<}\{0, 1, +, -, \cdot, {}^{-1}, <\} and corresponding axioms. Morphisms are order-preserving field embeddings. The real numbers ℝ\mathbb{R} are characterized as the unique (up to canonical isomorphism) Dedekind-complete ordered field, and any ordered-field morphism f:ℝ→Ff : \mathbb{R}\to F is necessarily an embedding (because ff preserves order and ℝ\mathbb{R} has no proper Dedekind cuts of ℚ\mathbb{Q}).

Proposition 33. Any two Dedekind-complete ordered fields are uniquely isomorphic, and the unique isomorphism preserves the constants 0,1,π,e,…0, 1, \pi, e, \dots as structural invariants.

Proof sketch. The order and field operations determine ℚ\mathbb{Q} inside any ordered field; Dedekind completion of ℚ\mathbb{Q} inside the field gives a copy of ℝ\mathbb{R}; the order-preserving map between two such copies is forced by the universal property of Dedekind completion. ◻

Group homomorphisms and conjugacy invariants

The theory TgrpT_{\mathrm{grp}} has signature {e,⋅,−1}\{e, \cdot, {}^{-1}\} and group axioms. A morphism of TgrpT_{\mathrm{grp}}-models is a group homomorphism. The structural invariants are: order, exponent, abelianization, conjugacy class structure, character table (for finite groups). The cardinality |G|∈ℕ|G| \in \mathbb{N} is the simplest such invariant; the character table χG\chi_G is a much richer one.

Remark 34 (Character tables and isospectrality). Two non-isomorphic groups can share the same character table; this rare phenomenon (the simplest example being a pair of pp-groups of order p5p^5 for some prime pp) shows that the character table is a genuine but partial structural invariant. It distinguishes most groups but does not separate all isomorphism classes. The structuralist program does not require that one invariant separate all classes; it requires only that every invariant respect isomorphism.

Models, Theories, and Toposes

ETCS and categorical foundations

Lawvere’s Elementary Theory of the Category of Sets (ETCS, 1964 ) reformulates set theory as a first-order axiomatization of 𝑺𝒆𝒕\mathbf{Set} as a (well-pointed) topos with a natural-numbers object (NNO) and choice. It is a categorical foundation: structural at its core because the axioms refer only to the categorical relations (terminal object, products, exponentials, subobject classifier, NNO).

Definition 35 (Topos). A topos is a category with finite limits, exponentials, and a subobject classifier Ω\Omega. A topos has all the categorical structure needed to interpret higher-order logic.

Definition 36 (NNO in a topos). A natural-numbers object in a topos ℰ\mathcal{E} is an object NN with 0:1→N0 : 1 \to N and s:N→N\mathrm{s}: N \to N such that for every (X,x0,f)(X, x_0, f) there is a unique h:N→Xh : N \to X with h∘0=x0h \circ 0 = x_0 and h∘s=f∘hh \circ \mathrm{s}= f \circ h.

Theorem 37 (Lawvere). ETCS is bi-interpretable with bounded Zermelo set theory plus a choice principle. Any model of ETCS contains a unique-up-to-isomorphism NNO, which serves as “the” natural numbers.

Models in toposes

A model of an algebraic theory in a topos ℰ\mathcal{E} is a finite-product-preserving functor 𝒯→ℰ\mathcal{T}\to \mathcal{E}. Different toposes give different “universes” in which to interpret arithmetic, but the structural invariants survive.

Example 38 (Sheaves). Let ℰ=Sh(X)\mathcal{E}= \mathrm{Sh}(X), the topos of sheaves on a topological space. A TsT_\mathrm{s}-model in ℰ\mathcal{E} is a sheaf of pointed unary structures. Locally these look like ordinary models in 𝑺𝒆𝒕\mathbf{Set}; globally they encode parameter dependence.

Example 39 (Effective topos). Hyland’s effective topos  is a topos in which the NNO carries a different “computability” structure: morphisms N→NN \to N are exactly the computable functions. The arithmetic invariants of NN (cardinality, orbit structure of s\mathrm{s}) coincide with those of the standard model, but the morphism structure is richer.

Ultraproducts and non-standard models

First-order Peano arithmetic (𝖯𝖠\mathsf{PA}) is weaker than the categorical NNO: it has non-standard models, in particular ultrapowers ℕω/𝒰\mathbb{N}^{\omega}/\mathcal{U} for non-principal ultrafilters 𝒰\mathcal{U}. These models satisfy every first-order arithmetic sentence true in ℕ\mathbb{N}, but contain “infinite” elements.

Theorem 40 (Łoś’ theorem). Let 𝒰\mathcal{U} be an ultrafilter on II and let (Mi)i∈I(M_i)_{i \in I} be a family of models of a first-order theory TT. The ultraproduct ∏Mi/𝒰\prod M_i / \mathcal{U} satisfies a first-order sentence φ\varphi iff {i:Mi⊨φ}∈𝒰\{i : M_i \models \varphi\} \in \mathcal{U}.

Remark 41. The categorical / NNO perspective forbids non-standard models: the universal property uniquely determines NN (up to canonical isomorphism). The first-order PA perspective admits them. The structuralist response: non-standard models satisfy a different theory than full second-order PA, and they should be analyzed as a different (richer) structure, not as “alternative natural numbers”.

Example 42 (Ultrapower and the standard cut). Let 𝒰\mathcal{U} be a non-principal ultrafilter on ω\omega and let ℕ*=ℕω/𝒰\mathbb{N}^* = \mathbb{N}^\omega/\mathcal{U}. The map ι:ℕ→ℕ*\iota : \mathbb{N}\to \mathbb{N}^* sending n↦[n,n,n,…]n \mapsto [n, n, n, \dots] is an embedding of structures, but its image is a proper initial segment (the “standard cut”) of ℕ*\mathbb{N}^*. The structural invariants of the standard cut — cardinality, orbit structure of s\mathrm{s}, the order-type ω\omega — coincide with those of ℕ\mathbb{N} itself; the “new” elements of ℕ*\mathbb{N}^* form a non-trivial extension that is consistent with first-order PA but not with the categorical NNO universal property. Thus the categorical / structural perspective explains why non-standard models exist (PA is too weak to pin down ℕ\mathbb{N} up to isomorphism) and why we discount them when speaking of “the” natural numbers (the structural invariants we care about live on the standard part).

Remark 43 (Skolem’s paradox). The same phenomenon underlies Skolem’s paradox: the Löwenheim–Skolem theorem produces countable models of ZFC even though ZFC proves the existence of uncountable sets. The resolution is that “uncountable” is not absolute — it depends on which model’s notion of bijection one uses. Structural invariants of ℕ\mathbb{N} and ℝ\mathbb{R} (cardinality, definable subsets, etc.) are model-relative for first-order theories, and only become absolute when the theory pins down the model up to isomorphism (as second-order PA and the categorical NNO do).

The Yoneda Embedding and Universal Properties

We pause to record how Yoneda and universal properties (the topics of papers 03 and 04 of this series) sit inside the structural perspective.

Theorem 44 (Yoneda lemma). For any locally small category 𝒞\mathcal{C}, object AA, and functor F:𝒞op→𝑺𝒆𝒕F : \mathcal{C}^\mathrm{op}\to \mathbf{Set}, Nat(Hom𝒞(−,A),F)≅F(A).\mathrm{Nat}(\mathrm{Hom}_\mathcal{C}(-, A), F) \cong F(A).

Corollary 45 (Yoneda embedding is fully faithful). The functor y:𝒞→[𝒞op,𝑺𝒆𝒕]y : \mathcal{C}\to [\mathcal{C}^\mathrm{op}, \mathbf{Set}] sending A↦Hom𝒞(−,A)A \mapsto \mathrm{Hom}_\mathcal{C}(-, A) is fully faithful: Hom𝒞(A,B)≅Nat(yA,yB)\mathrm{Hom}_\mathcal{C}(A, B) \cong\mathrm{Nat}(yA, yB).

Corollary 46 (Identity by relations). A≅BA \cong B in 𝒞\mathcal{C} iff yA≅yByA \cong yB in [𝒞op,𝑺𝒆𝒕][\mathcal{C}^\mathrm{op}, \mathbf{Set}], iff Hom(−,A)≅Hom(−,B)\mathrm{Hom}(-, A) \cong\mathrm{Hom}(-, B) as presheaves.

46 is the Yoneda formulation of structuralism: an object is determined (up to canonical isomorphism) by its system of incoming maps. “5858” is its representable functor HomMod⁡(Ts)(−,58)\mathrm{Hom}_{\operatorname{Mod}(T_\mathrm{s})}(-, 58) in the category of finite cyclic-or-linear successor structures.

Proposition 47 (Universal properties are structural). Any object characterized by a universal property is determined up to canonical isomorphism. Therefore, the value of any property of universally characterized objects depends only on the isomorphism class.

The categorical / structural perspective of 4 and the Yoneda perspective of 8 agree: both say that mathematical content is preserved by isomorphism and that this is the right level of identity.

Equivalence-Invariance and Transport

We now reach the bridge between the categorical / structural perspective and HoTT. The key technical fact is that HoTT — the type-theoretic side of the Curry–Howard–Lambek correspondence  — validates an internal version of 14 and 26: the structure identity principle (SIP).

Univalence

Definition 48 (Univalence axiom, Voevodsky ). For types A,BA, B in a universe 𝒰\mathcal{U}, the canonical map idtoeqv:(A=𝒰B)→(A≃B)\mathrm{idtoeqv} : (A =_{\mathcal{U}} B) \to (A \simeq B) is itself an equivalence.

Univalence asserts that “equality of types” and “equivalence of types” are themselves equivalent: any equivalence can be transported into an identification, and any identification gives back the corresponding equivalence.

Theorem 49 (Transport). Given P:𝒰→𝒰P : \mathcal{U} \to \mathcal{U} and p:A=𝒰Bp : A =_\mathcal{U} B, there is a function transportP(p):P(A)→P(B)\mathrm{transport}^P(p) : P(A) \to P(B). Moreover, transportP(reflA)=idP(A)\mathrm{transport}^P(\mathrm{refl}_A) = \mathrm{id}_{P(A)}.

Structure identity principle

Theorem 50 (Structure identity principle, schematic form). Let 𝒮\mathcal{S} be a notion of structure (e.g. a TsT_\mathrm{s}-model in HoTT) defined by a Σ\Sigma-type ΣX:𝒰F(X)\Sigma_{X : \mathcal{U}}\, F(X) where FF is a structure of finite signature on the carrier XX. Then identification of structures is canonically equivalent to structure-preserving equivalence of carriers: ((X,s)=ΣXF(X)(X′,s′))≃(Σf:X≃X′f preserves s,s′).\big( (X, s) =_{\Sigma_{X} F(X)} (X', s') \big) \;\simeq\; \big(\Sigma_{f : X \simeq X'}\, f \text{ preserves } s, s' \big).

Remark 51. 50 is proved level-by-level for set-level structures in the HoTT Book , Chapter 9, and generalized to higher levels by Coquand–Danielsson and others . It says: in HoTT, two structures of the same kind are equal iff they are isomorphic in the structure-preserving sense.

Theorem 52 (Equivalence-invariance / transportability). In HoTT, every property P:𝒮→𝒰P : \mathcal{S} \to \mathcal{U} of structures of a given kind 𝒮\mathcal{S} is automatically invariant under structure-preserving equivalence: if (X,s)≃𝒮(X′,s′)(X, s) \simeq_\mathcal{S} (X', s') then P(X,s)≃P(X′,s′)P(X, s) \simeq P(X', s').

Proof. By 50, structure-preserving equivalence is identification. By the action of PP on identifications (the J-rule, alias path induction), identifications are transported by PP. Combine. ◻

52 is the internal counterpart of the meta-theoretic 14. Where the latter says “one ought to write only structural definitions” as a methodological discipline, the former says “every definition you can write is structural” as a theorem of the foundations. Univalence is the principle that promotes the meta-condition into an internal one.

Categorification and Decategorification

From sets to groupoids

Definition 53 (Groupoid). A groupoid is a category in which every morphism is invertible.

Definition 54 (π0\pi_0, set of connected components). For a groupoid GG, π0(G)\pi_0(G) is the set of isomorphism classes of objects.

Example 55. Let G58G_{58} be a groupoid with 5858 objects {0,1,…,57}\{0, 1, \dots, 57\} and only identity morphisms. Then π0(G58)={{0},…,{57}}\pi_0(G_{58}) = \{\{0\}, \dots, \{57\}\}, a 5858-element set. Cardinality is preserved: |π0(G58)|=58|\pi_0(G_{58})| = 58.

Example 56 (A connected groupoid with one component). Let GBℤ/nℤG_{B\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}} be the groupoid with one object and automorphism group ℤ/nℤ\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}. Then π0(GBℤ/nℤ)\pi_0(G_{B\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}}) is a one-element set.

Cardinality of groupoids

For finite groupoids, a finer invariant than |π0||\pi_0| is the groupoid cardinality : |G|gpd=∑[x]∈π0(G)1|Aut(x)|.|G|_{\mathrm{gpd}} = \sum_{[x] \in \pi_0(G)} \frac{1}{|\mathrm{Aut}(x)|}.

Example 57. |G58|gpd=58|G_{58}|_{\mathrm{gpd}} = 58, since each automorphism group is trivial. |GBℤ/nℤ|gpd=1/n|G_{B\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}}|_{\mathrm{gpd}} = 1/n, decategorifying to a rational invariant.

Decategorification, Euler characteristic, KK-theory

Decategorification is a general process of descending from higher-dimensional structure to numerical content:

  • π0\pi_0 from groupoids to sets (and then |⋅||\cdot| to cardinals);

  • Euler characteristic χ\chi from CW-complexes to integers;

  • K0K_0 from a triangulated category to its Grothendieck group.

Definition 58 (Euler characteristic of finite groupoids). For a finite groupoid GG, the Euler characteristic is χ(G)=|G|gpd\chi(G) = |G|_{\mathrm{gpd}}.

Theorem 59 (Decategorification preserves equivalence). Equivalent groupoids G≃G′G \simeq G' satisfy π0(G)≅π0(G′)\pi_0(G) \cong\pi_0(G') and |G|gpd=|G′|gpd|G|_{\mathrm{gpd}} = |G'|_{\mathrm{gpd}}.

Proof. Equivalences induce bijections on isomorphism classes, hence equal π0\pi_0 and equal sums in the cardinality formula. ◻

Numbers in physics

The same invariance principle reappears in physics. Gauge invariants and conserved quantities are exactly those numbers preserved under the symmetries of a system; what survives a gauge transformation is structural content, what fails to survive is gauge artifact.

Example 60. Mass, charge, energy, momentum, angular momentum, spin: each is a Noether-conserved quantity associated to a symmetry of the action functional. The conserved quantity is the structural invariant; the dynamical trajectory is the representation.

Example 61 (Spin as a representation-theoretic invariant). The total spin ss of a quantum-mechanical particle is the index of an irreducible representation of SU(2)\mathrm{SU}(2). Two systems whose Hilbert-space representations of the rotation group are isomorphic carry the same spin: ss is an invariant of the representation, not of any concrete realization. This is the same structuralist move applied at a different sort of theory — a theory of representations of a Lie group rather than of a Lawvere theory.

Remark 62. The structuralist principle “numbers are invariants” is therefore not specific to mathematics. It is the same idea that physics calls gauge invariance, that representation theory calls character / dimension / trace, and that K-theory calls stable equivalence class. In each case the formal apparatus differs, but the conceptual move is the same: discard the representation-dependent content; retain only the structural content.

Beyond decategorification: counting beyond cardinality

Decategorification can carry more refined information than ordinary cardinality. We collect three increasingly fine invariants extracted from a (suitable) finite groupoid GG:

  1. |π0(G)|∈ℕ|\pi_0(G)| \in \mathbb{N} — the number of isomorphism classes of objects;

  2. |G|gpd∈ℚ≥0|G|_{\mathrm{gpd}} \in \mathbb{Q}_{\geq 0} — the groupoid cardinality, weighted by automorphism group orders;

  3. the homotopy type of the geometric realization |N(G)||N(G)| — a topological invariant containing all πn(G)\pi_n(G).

Example 63 (Combinatorial species and exponential generating functions). A combinatorial species is a functor FinSetbij→𝑺𝒆𝒕\mathrm{FinSet}_{\mathrm{bij}} \to \mathbf{Set}. Its exponential generating function is the sum F(z)=∑n≥0|F([n])/Sn|znn!,F(z) = \sum_{n \geq 0} |F([n])/S_n| \, \frac{z^n}{n!}, encoding all the species’ counting data as a power series. The series is invariant under natural isomorphism of species: two equivalent species give the same EGF.

Theorem 64 (Invariance of decategorification). Let Φ:𝑮𝒑𝒅fin→ℰ\Phi : \mathbf{Gpd}_{\mathrm{fin}} \to \mathcal{E} be any functor from the 22-category of finite groupoids to a category ℰ\mathcal{E}. For equivalent groupoids G≃G′G \simeq G', Φ(G)≅Φ(G′)\Phi(G) \cong\Phi(G') in ℰ\mathcal{E}.

Proof. Φ\Phi sends equivalences (which are the morphisms of the underlying 11-groupoid of 𝑮𝒑𝒅fin\mathbf{Gpd}_{\mathrm{fin}}) to isomorphisms. ◻

The previous theorem subsumes all the decategorification examples: π0\pi_0, |⋅|gpd|\cdot|_{\mathrm{gpd}}, Euler characteristic, K0K_0 are each instances of this invariance.

Results

We collect the principal results of the paper.

Theorem 65 (Main, restatement). Let 𝒯\mathcal{T} be a Lawvere theory and let M,M′:𝒯→𝑺𝒆𝒕M, M' : \mathcal{T}\to \mathbf{Set} be two models. If M≅M′M \cong M' in Mod⁡(𝒯)\operatorname{Mod}(\mathcal{T}), then for every definable invariant II (23), I(M)≅I(M′)I(M) \cong I(M'). In the special case ℰ=𝑺𝒆𝒕\mathcal{E}= \mathbf{Set} and I(M)I(M) a finite set, |I(M)|=|I(M′)||I(M)| = |I(M')|.

Theorem 66 (Concrete instance). The von Neumann and Zermelo finite ordinals up to height 44 are isomorphic as TsT_\mathrm{s}-models in 𝑺𝒆𝒕\mathbf{Set} but not equal as sets in ZFC. Every numerical invariant agrees on both. This agreement is verified computationally by the Haskell implementation in 11.

Theorem 67 (HoTT promotion, restatement of 52). In HoTT with the univalence axiom, every property PP of structures of a given kind is automatically invariant under structure-preserving equivalence. Hence the meta-theoretic discipline of writing only structural definitions becomes the internal theorem “every definition is structural”.

Theorem 68 (Decategorification preserves invariants). For finite groupoids G≃G′G \simeq G', π0(G)≅π0(G′)\pi_0(G) \cong\pi_0(G') as sets, |π0(G)|=|π0(G′)||\pi_0(G)| = |\pi_0(G')| as natural numbers, and |G|gpd=|G′|gpd|G|_{\mathrm{gpd}} = |G'|_{\mathrm{gpd}} as rational numbers.

Discussion

Implications

The invariance theorem (26) is technically modest — it says functors send isomorphisms to isomorphisms — but its philosophical force is large. Combined with 14, it cashes out the structuralist slogan “numbers are positions” in a fully formal way: a position in a structure is exactly a Mod⁡(𝒯)\operatorname{Mod}(\mathcal{T})-invariant of finite power, and “5858” picks out the same thing in any model up to canonical isomorphism.

In HoTT (9), the same principle is internalized: the meta-condition becomes a theorem. This is one of the two senses in which univalence is foundational: it eliminates the gap between “structural in principle” and “provably structural”.

Alternative categorical foundations

ETCS is not the only category-theoretic foundation for mathematics; it is the one closest to traditional set theory in expressive power. Other proposals deserve mention:

  • ETCS / well-pointed topos with NNO and choice : a first-order axiomatization of the category of sets. Bi-interpretable with bounded Zermelo set theory plus choice.

  • SEAR (sets, elements, and relations): a Lawvere-style structural set theory developed by Shulman, organized around relations rather than functions.

  • FOLDS (first-order logic with dependent sorts): Makkai’s framework in which equality between objects of distinct sorts is not a well-formed formula, automatically enforcing structural invariance.

  • HoTT / Univalent Foundations : dependent type theory with the univalence axiom, in which structural invariance is a theorem of the foundations rather than a syntactic discipline.

A common thread runs through all four: the formal apparatus is set up so that one cannot — or at least, one should not — formulate a non-structural property. Where ETCS achieves this by using only categorical primitives, FOLDS by sort discipline, and HoTT by univalence, the philosophical content is the same: only structural content is mathematically meaningful.

Limitations

We have presented the structural perspective at the level of finite Lawvere theories interpreted in 𝑺𝒆𝒕\mathbf{Set}. The full strength of the perspective requires:

  • finite-product theories with sorts (multi-sorted Lawvere theories), which suffice for first-order universal algebra;

  • finite-limit theories (essentially algebraic theories), which suffice for partial operations like division;

  • topos-theoretic semantics for full first-order logic (geometric / coherent theories);

  • dependent type theories (Martin-Löf, HoTT) for the most expressive case.

The invariance principle survives at every level: the categorical content of the principle does not depend on the size of the theory.

Connection to the synthesis

This is paper 0606 of a six-paper series. The synthesis paper consolidates the per-topic results into a single thesis: that the levels in 1 (rows 3–6) are not analogous but literally the same, identified up to equivalence, in HoTT with univalence. The present paper has prepared the ground for that consolidation by:

  1. supplying the formal version of structuralism (functorial semantics);

  2. proving the invariance theorem on which the consolidation depends;

  3. identifying univalence as the principle that promotes the invariance from a meta-theorem to an internal theorem.

Conclusion

A number is what survives every change of representation that preserves the structure. We have made this slogan precise: numbers are definable invariants of TsT_\mathrm{s}-models, equivalently functors from the underlying groupoid of Mod⁡(Ts)\operatorname{Mod}(T_\mathrm{s}) to whatever target category one chooses to extract numerical content into. Two models that are isomorphic as TsT_\mathrm{s}-models give the same answer to every such question. The Haskell code makes the principle executable for the von Neumann / Zermelo example.

The deeper claim is that this principle is the same one that universal properties express, that the Yoneda embedding internalizes, and that HoTT with univalence makes literal: structural identity is identity. The synthesis paper develops the consequences.

Future work

Three directions deserve attention:

  1. Higher Lawvere theories: extend the functorial-semantics framework to (∞,1)(\infty,1)-Lawvere theories and verify that the invariance theorem persists at every level of homotopy.

  2. Modal structuralism in HoTT: formalize Hellman’s modal structuralism using the modalities of HoTT (open / closed / monadic modalities) and compare to the absolute version.

  3. Decategorification of physical theories: apply the invariance lens to gauge theories and conserved quantities, and clarify the relationship between structural invariance and Noether’s theorem.
