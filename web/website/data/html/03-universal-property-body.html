Introduction

Position in the Six-Level Ladder

The Univalent Correspondence series organizes the question “what is a number?” into six levels of abstraction . Paper 01 examined the naive level (numerals as syntax). Paper 02 examined the set-theoretic level (von Neumann ordinals; Benacerraf’s identification problem ). The present paper takes the next step. We replace “5858 is the set {0,1,…,57}\{0, 1, \dots, 57\}” with the encoding-independent assertion:

5858 is the position obtained by iterating 𝗌𝗎𝖼𝖼\mathsf{succ} exactly 5858 times from 00 in any initial successor structure.

Two such initial structures are connected by a unique isomorphism, so the position labelled 5858 in one corresponds to the position labelled 5858 in the other. The number is not a set; it is a node in a universal diagram.

What Counts as a Universal Property

A universal property of an object UU in a category 𝒞\mathcal{C} is a description of UU as the unique object (up to unique isomorphism) carrying a specified piece of data such that, for every other object carrying analogous data, there is a unique structure-preserving morphism out of (or into) UU . The natural numbers, the real numbers, the field of rationals, the circle, the exponential function: each admits such a presentation, and each becomes encoding-independent thereby.

The strength of the universal-property perspective is threefold:

  1. Encoding-independence. The definition refers to morphisms, not elements; the internal constitution of UU is opaque.

  2. Compositionality. Universal morphisms compose as morphisms; structures built by universal property compose accordingly.

  3. Computational content. The unique morphism guaranteed by the universal property is the recursion (or corecursion) combinator, directly executable as a program.

The Critique We Will Address

A familiar objection: “up to unique isomorphism” still tolerates many isomorphic copies; choosing one is still a choice. The structuralist response, due to Lawvere and developed by Awodey , is that the choice is semantically irrelevant because every property of mathematical interest is invariant under isomorphism (the principle of structuralism). HoTT will later make this principle definitional via univalence (Paper 5). The universal property is the gateway: it teaches us to live with “up to unique iso” as the appropriate identity criterion.

Mathematical Framework

Categories, Functors, Pointed Dynamical Systems

Definition 1 (Category). A category 𝒞\mathcal{C} consists of a class of objects, for every pair A,BA, B a set Hom𝒞(A,B)\mathrm{Hom}_{\mathcal{C}}(A,B) of morphisms, an associative composition ∘\circ, and identities idA\mathrm{id}_A.

Definition 2 (Pointed dynamical system). Let 𝒞\mathcal{C} be a category with a chosen terminal object 11. A pointed dynamical system in 𝒞\mathcal{C} is a triple (X,x0,f)(X, x_0, f) where X∈𝒞X \in \mathcal{C}, x0:1→Xx_0 \colon 1 \to X, and f:X→Xf \colon X \to X. A morphism of pointed dynamical systems (X,x0,f)→(Y,y0,g)(X, x_0, f) \to (Y, y_0, g) is a morphism h:X→Yh \colon X \to Y in 𝒞\mathcal{C} such that h∘x0=y0andh∘f=g∘h.\begin{equation} h \circ x_0 = y_0 \quad \text{and} \quad h \circ f = g \circ h. \label{eq:pds-morphism} \end{equation} We denote the resulting category 𝑷𝒕𝑬𝒏𝒅𝒐(𝒞)\mathbf{PtEndo}(\mathcal{C}) (“pointed endo-systems in 𝒞\mathcal{C}”). When 𝒞=𝑺𝒆𝒕\mathcal{C} = \mathbf{Set} we drop the parameter and write 𝑷𝒕𝑬𝒏𝒅𝒐\mathbf{PtEndo}. The category 𝑷𝒕𝑬𝒏𝒅𝒐(𝒞)\mathbf{PtEndo}(\mathcal{C}) is canonically isomorphic to the category of algebras for the endofunctor FX=1+XF X = 1 + X in 𝒞\mathcal{C}: the basepoint x0:1→Xx_0 \colon 1 \to X and the dynamics f:X→Xf \colon X \to X assemble (via the universal property of the coproduct) into a single algebra map [x0,f]:1+X→X[x_0, f] \colon 1 + X \to X, and conversely.

The two equations of [eq:pds-morphism] say exactly that the diagrams $$\begin{tikzcd} 1 \arrow[r, "x_0"] \arrow[dr, "y_0"'] & X \arrow[d, "h"] \\ & Y \end{tikzcd} \qquad \begin{tikzcd} X \arrow[r, "f"] \arrow[d, "h"'] & X \arrow[d, "h"] \\ Y \arrow[r, "g"'] & Y \end{tikzcd}$$ commute. The first asserts “hh preserves the basepoint”; the second asserts “hh commutes with the dynamics.”

Definition 3 (Initial object). An object II of 𝒞\mathcal{C} is initial if for every X∈𝒞X \in \mathcal{C} there is a unique morphism !X:I→X!_X \colon I \to X.

Lemma 4 (Rigidity of initial objects). If II and I′I' are initial in 𝒞\mathcal{C}, there is a unique isomorphism I→∼I′I \xrightarrow{\sim} I'. In particular, the automorphism group of an initial object is trivial.

Proof. By initiality of II there is a unique u:I→I′u \colon I \to I'; by initiality of I′I' there is a unique v:I′→Iv \colon I' \to I. Both v∘uv \circ u and idI\mathrm{id}_I are morphisms I→II \to I, hence equal by uniqueness; similarly u∘v=idI′u \circ v = \mathrm{id}_{I'}. ◻

Endofunctor Algebras and Initial Algebras

Definition 5 (FF-algebra). Let F:𝒞→𝒞F \colon \mathcal{C} \to \mathcal{C} be an endofunctor. An FF-algebra is a pair (X,α)(X, \alpha) with α:FX→X\alpha \colon F X \to X. A morphism of FF-algebras (X,α)→(Y,β)(X, \alpha) \to (Y, \beta) is h:X→Yh \colon X \to Y with h∘α=β∘Fhh \circ \alpha = \beta \circ Fh. The category of FF-algebras is denoted F-𝑨𝒍𝒈F\text{-}\mathbf{Alg}.

A pointed dynamical system (X,x0,f)(X, x_0, f) is precisely an algebra for the endofunctor FX=1+XF X = 1 + X in 𝑺𝒆𝒕\mathbf{Set} (or any category with finite coproducts and a terminal object): the algebra map [x0,f]:1+X→X[x_0, f] \colon 1 + X \to X packages the basepoint and the dynamics into a single arrow. Algebras for FX=1+XF X = 1 + X are called successor algebras or pointed endo-systems.

Lemma 6 (Lambek). Let F:𝒞→𝒞F \colon \mathcal{C} \to \mathcal{C} be an endofunctor and let (I,ι)(I, \iota) be an initial FF-algebra. Then ι:FI→I\iota \colon F I \to I is an isomorphism.

Proof. Apply FF to obtain Fι:FFI→FIF\iota \colon F F I \to F I. The pair (FI,Fι)(F I, F \iota) is an FF-algebra, hence by initiality there is a unique morphism u:I→FIu \colon I \to F I of FF-algebras. Then ι∘u:I→I\iota \circ u \colon I \to I is an algebra morphism, and so is idI\mathrm{id}_I; by uniqueness ι∘u=idI\iota \circ u = \mathrm{id}_I. Conversely, since uu is by construction an algebra morphism (I,ι)→(FI,Fι)(I, \iota) \to (F I, F\iota), we have u∘ι=Fι∘Fu=F(ι∘u)u \circ \iota = F\iota \circ Fu = F(\iota \circ u). Substituting the equation ι∘u=idI\iota \circ u = \mathrm{id}_I just established gives u∘ι=F(idI)=idFIu \circ \iota = F(\mathrm{id}_I) = \mathrm{id}_{F I}. Hence ι\iota and uu are mutually inverse, and ι\iota is an isomorphism. ◻

Lambek’s lemma is the categorical analogue of Peano’s third axiom (“𝗌𝗎𝖼𝖼\mathsf{succ} is injective and avoids 00”): the algebra structure ι=[0,𝗌𝗎𝖼𝖼]:1+ℕ→ℕ\iota = [0, \mathsf{succ}] \colon 1 + \mathbb{N}\to \mathbb{N} is a bijection, so 00 and 𝗌𝗎𝖼𝖼(n)\mathsf{succ}(n) are distinct, and 𝗌𝗎𝖼𝖼\mathsf{succ} is injective.

The Natural Numbers Object

Definition and Universal Property

Definition 7 (Natural numbers object). A natural numbers object (NNO) in a category 𝒞\mathcal{C} with terminal object 11 is a triple (N,0:1→N,𝗌𝗎𝖼𝖼:N→N)(N, 0 \colon 1 \to N, \mathsf{succ}\colon N \to N) that is initial in 𝑷𝒕𝑬𝒏𝒅𝒐(𝒞)\mathbf{PtEndo}(\mathcal{C}). Equivalently, (N,[0,𝗌𝗎𝖼𝖼])(N, [0, \mathsf{succ}]) is initial among algebras for the endofunctor FX=1+XF X = 1 + X.

Remark 8 (Notation for the NNO). We use the symbol NN for a generic NNO in an arbitrary category 𝒞\mathcal{C}, and ℕ\mathbb{N} specifically when 𝒞=𝑺𝒆𝒕\mathcal{C} = \mathbf{Set} (the standard natural numbers). The two notations refer to the same structural object up to canonical isomorphism (Lemma 4); we use ℕ\mathbb{N} in the abstract, in informal discussion, and from Section 5 onward, where the working category is 𝑺𝒆𝒕\mathbf{Set} (or its enrichment for analysis).

Theorem 9 (Universal property of the NNO). Let (N,0,𝗌𝗎𝖼𝖼)(N, 0, \mathsf{succ}) be an NNO in 𝒞\mathcal{C}. For every pointed dynamical system (X,x0,f)(X, x_0, f) in 𝒞\mathcal{C}, there exists a unique morphism h:N→Xh \colon N \to X such that h∘0=x0andh∘𝗌𝗎𝖼𝖼=f∘h.\begin{equation} h \circ 0 = x_0 \quad \text{and} \quad h \circ \mathsf{succ}= f \circ h. \label{eq:nno-up} \end{equation}

Proof. This is the definition of initiality in 𝑷𝒕𝑬𝒏𝒅𝒐(𝒞)\mathbf{PtEndo}(\mathcal{C}) rewritten via Definition 2. Existence and uniqueness of hh together with the two commuting diagrams are jointly the assertion that (X,x0,f)(X, x_0, f) is an object of 𝑷𝒕𝑬𝒏𝒅𝒐(𝒞)\mathbf{PtEndo}(\mathcal{C}) and hh is the unique morphism from (N,0,𝗌𝗎𝖼𝖼)(N, 0, \mathsf{succ}). ◻

The Recursion Combinator

The unique map hh guaranteed by Theorem 9 is the primitive recursion combinator. Writing rec(x0,f):=h\mathrm{rec}(x_0, f) := h, equation [eq:nno-up] unfolds as rec(x0,f)(0)=x0,rec(x0,f)(𝗌𝗎𝖼𝖼n)=f(rec(x0,f)(n)).\begin{equation} \mathrm{rec}(x_0, f)(0) = x_0, \qquad \mathrm{rec}(x_0, f)(\mathsf{succ}n) = f\bigl(\mathrm{rec}(x_0, f)(n)\bigr). \label{eq:rec-eqs} \end{equation} These are the recursive equations defining iteration.

Example 10 (Addition). Define add:ℕ→(ℕ→ℕ)\mathrm{add} \colon \mathbb{N}\to (\mathbb{N}\to \mathbb{N}) by add:=rec(idℕ,λg.𝗌𝗎𝖼𝖼∘g)\mathrm{add} := \mathrm{rec}\bigl(\mathrm{id}_\mathbb{N},\ \lambda g.\ \mathsf{succ}\circ g\bigr), where the codomain is the function space (assuming 𝒞\mathcal{C} is cartesian closed). Then add(0)=idℕ\mathrm{add}(0) = \mathrm{id}_\mathbb{N} and add(𝗌𝗎𝖼𝖼n)(m)=𝗌𝗎𝖼𝖼(add(n)(m))\mathrm{add}(\mathsf{succ}n)(m) = \mathsf{succ}(\mathrm{add}(n)(m)), recovering Peano addition.

Example 11 (Multiplication). Define mul:ℕ→(ℕ→ℕ)\mathrm{mul} \colon \mathbb{N}\to (\mathbb{N}\to \mathbb{N}) by mul:=rec(const0,λg.λm.add(m)(g(m))).\mathrm{mul} := \mathrm{rec}\bigl(\mathrm{const}_0,\ \lambda g.\ \lambda m.\ \mathrm{add}(m)(g(m))\bigr). The base case mul(0)\mathrm{mul}(0) is the constant zero function; the step takes a function gg representing “multiplication by nn” and returns the function m↦m+g(m)m \mapsto m + g(m), representing “multiplication by 𝗌𝗎𝖼𝖼n\mathsf{succ}n.” Each arithmetic operation is a single recursion combinator instance.

Example 12 (Position 58). The element 58∈N58 \in N is 𝗌𝗎𝖼𝖼58(0)\mathsf{succ}^{58}(0). By Theorem 9, in any pointed dynamical system (X,x0,f)(X, x_0, f), the corresponding “58th iteration” is rec(x0,f)(58)=f58(x0)\mathrm{rec}(x_0, f)(58) = f^{58}(x_0). Two NNOs (N,0,𝗌𝗎𝖼𝖼)(N, 0, \mathsf{succ}) and (N′,0′,𝗌𝗎𝖼𝖼′)(N', 0', \mathsf{succ}') admit unique mutually-inverse morphisms (Lemma 4); these match 𝗌𝗎𝖼𝖼58(0)\mathsf{succ}^{58}(0) to (𝗌𝗎𝖼𝖼′)58(0′)(\mathsf{succ}')^{58}(0'). The number 58 is exactly this trans-NNO equivalence class.

The Iteration Principle versus Primitive Recursion

Theorem 9 as stated gives the iteration principle: from (x0,f)(x_0, f) produce a function ℕ→X\mathbb{N}\to X. Genuine primitive recursion allows the recursive call to depend on the index: g(0)=x0,g(𝗌𝗎𝖼𝖼n)=k(n,g(n)).\begin{equation} g(0) = x_0, \qquad g(\mathsf{succ}n) = k(n, g(n)). \label{eq:prim-rec} \end{equation}

Proposition 13 (Primitive recursion from iteration). In any cartesian category with NNO, primitive recursion as in [eq:prim-rec] is derivable from iteration as in [eq:rec-eqs].

Proof. Define g:=π2∘g̃:ℕ→Xg := \pi_2 \circ \widetilde{g} \colon \mathbb{N}\to X. Then g(0)=x0g(0) = x_0 and g(𝗌𝗎𝖼𝖼n)=k(n,g(n))g(\mathsf{succ}n) = k(n, g(n)). Uniqueness of gg follows from uniqueness of g̃\widetilde{g} and the fact that π1∘g̃=idℕ\pi_1 \circ \widetilde{g} = \mathrm{id}_\mathbb{N} by induction. ◻

Comparison with Peano Arithmetic

Second-Order Peano Axioms

The second-order Peano axioms are:

  1. 00 is a natural number.

  2. For every natural nn, 𝗌𝗎𝖼𝖼n\mathsf{succ}n is a natural number.

  3. For every nn, 𝗌𝗎𝖼𝖼n≠0\mathsf{succ}n \neq 0.

  4. For all m,nm, n, 𝗌𝗎𝖼𝖼m=𝗌𝗎𝖼𝖼n⇒m=n\mathsf{succ}m = \mathsf{succ}n \Rightarrow m = n.

  5. (Induction) For every subset S⊆ℕS \subseteq \mathbb{N} with 0∈S0 \in S and SS closed under 𝗌𝗎𝖼𝖼\mathsf{succ}, S=ℕS = \mathbb{N}.

Theorem 15 (Equivalence with NNO). A structure (N,0,𝗌𝗎𝖼𝖼)(N, 0, \mathsf{succ}) in 𝑺𝒆𝒕\mathbf{Set} satisfies P1–P5 if and only if it is a natural numbers object.

Sketch. (⇒)(\Rightarrow) Suppose (N,0,𝗌𝗎𝖼𝖼)(N, 0, \mathsf{succ}) is an NNO, i.e., initial in 𝑷𝒕𝑬𝒏𝒅𝒐(𝑺𝒆𝒕)\mathbf{PtEndo}(\mathbf{Set}). Since (N,[0,𝗌𝗎𝖼𝖼])(N, [0, \mathsf{succ}]) is the initial algebra for FX=1+XF X = 1 + X, Lambek’s lemma (Lemma 6) gives that [0,𝗌𝗎𝖼𝖼]:1+N→N[0, \mathsf{succ}] \colon 1 + N \to N is an isomorphism. Reading off the components: 00 and 𝗌𝗎𝖼𝖼\mathsf{succ} have disjoint images (P3, “0∉im𝗌𝗎𝖼𝖼0 \notin \mathrm{im}\,\mathsf{succ}”) and 𝗌𝗎𝖼𝖼\mathsf{succ} is injective (P4). For P5: given a subset S⊆NS \subseteq N with 0∈S0 \in S and closed under 𝗌𝗎𝖼𝖼\mathsf{succ}, the inclusion S↪NS \hookrightarrow N together with the restricted basepoint and successor makes SS a pointed-endo-system; the universal property gives a unique h:N→Sh \colon N \to S with h(0)=0h(0) = 0 and h∘𝗌𝗎𝖼𝖼|S=𝗌𝗎𝖼𝖼∘hh \circ \mathsf{succ}|_S = \mathsf{succ}\circ h, and the composite S↪N→hSS \hookrightarrow N \xrightarrow{h} S together with idN:N→N\mathrm{id}_N \colon N \to N both fit into the universal property at target (N,0,𝗌𝗎𝖼𝖼)(N, 0, \mathsf{succ}), forcing h=idNh = \mathrm{id}_N as a function and hence S=NS = N.

First-Order PA and Non-Standard Models

First-order Peano arithmetic (PA) replaces the second-order P5 with a schema: induction holds for every formula φ\varphi in the language of arithmetic. By the Lowenheim–Skolem theorem PA has non-standard models of every infinite cardinality ; these contain “infinite” elements not reachable from 00 by finitely many applications of 𝗌𝗎𝖼𝖼\mathsf{succ}.

Theorem 16 (Categorical pinning down). The standard model is the unique (up to unique isomorphism) NNO in 𝑺𝒆𝒕\mathbf{Set}. Non-standard models of PA are not NNOs in 𝑺𝒆𝒕\mathbf{Set} because they fail the universal property: there exist pointed dynamical systems (X,x0,f)(X, x_0, f) for which the would-be morphism hh is non-unique on the non-standard part.

The categorical NNO thus offers what first-order axiomatization cannot: a structural criterion that rejects non-standard inhabitants.

Other Numbers and Spaces by Universal Property

We now extend the universal-property perspective beyond ℕ\mathbb{N}.

The Integers as the Free Group on One Generator

Definition 17 (Universal property of ℤ\mathbb{Z}). ℤ\mathbb{Z} is the free group on one generator. Concretely: for any group GG and any element g∈Gg \in G, there exists a unique group homomorphism φg:ℤ→G\varphi_g \colon \mathbb{Z}\to G such that φg(1)=g\varphi_g(1) = g.

The unique map φg\varphi_g is n↦gnn \mapsto g^n. The universal property says equivalently that the forgetful functor 𝑮𝒓𝒑→𝑺𝒆𝒕*\mathbf{Grp} \to \mathbf{Set}_* from groups to pointed sets has ℤ\mathbb{Z} as the value of its left adjoint at the singleton-pointed set.

The Rationals as the Field of Fractions

Definition 18 (Universal property of ℚ\mathbb{Q}). ℚ\mathbb{Q} together with the canonical embedding ι:ℤ→ℚ\iota \colon \mathbb{Z}\to \mathbb{Q} is the field of fractions of ℤ\mathbb{Z}: it is initial among pairs (K,ȷ)(K, \jmath) where KK is a field, ȷ:ℤ→K\jmath \colon \mathbb{Z}\to K a ring homomorphism, and every nonzero element of ℤ\mathbb{Z} is sent to a unit. For any such (K,ȷ)(K, \jmath) there is a unique field homomorphism ȷ‾:ℚ→K\bar{\jmath} \colon \mathbb{Q}\to K with ȷ‾∘ι=ȷ\bar{\jmath} \circ \iota = \jmath.

The unique extension is determined by ȷ‾(p/q)=ȷ(p)ȷ(q)−1\bar{\jmath}(p/q) = \jmath(p) \jmath(q)^{-1}. The construction (equivalence classes of pairs (p,q)(p, q) with q≠0q \neq 0) is one realization of the universal object; the universal property captures the structure encoding-independently.

The Reals as the Dedekind-Complete Ordered Field

Theorem 19 (Universal property of ℝ\mathbb{R}). Up to unique order-preserving isomorphism, ℝ\mathbb{R} is the unique Dedekind-complete totally ordered field. Equivalently, ℝ\mathbb{R} is the terminal object in the category of Archimedean ordered fields with order-preserving embeddings (or initial in the category of ordered fields with an order-preserving Cauchy-completion comparison map).

Sketch. Existence: any of the standard constructions (Dedekind cuts, Cauchy sequences modulo equivalence). Uniqueness: given two Dedekind-complete ordered fields ℝ1,ℝ2\mathbb{R}_1, \mathbb{R}_2, both contain canonical copies of ℚ\mathbb{Q}; the order-preserving extension ℚ→ℝ2\mathbb{Q}\to \mathbb{R}_2 defined on ℝ1\mathbb{R}_1 via suprema of cuts is forced by completeness, and is bijective by Archimedeanness. ◻

The Exponential and the Number ee

The exponential function admits multiple universal characterizations.

Theorem 20 (Universal property of exp\exp). The exponential function exp⁡:ℝ→ℝ>0\exp \colon \mathbb{R}\to \mathbb{R}_{>0} is the unique smooth function f:ℝ→ℝf \colon \mathbb{R}\to \mathbb{R} satisfying

  1. f(0)=1f(0) = 1;

  2. f′(x)=f(x)f'(x) = f(x) for all xx.

Equivalently, exp\exp is the unique continuous group homomorphism (ℝ,+)→(ℝ>0,⋅)(\mathbb{R}, +) \to (\mathbb{R}_{>0}, \cdot) whose derivative at 00 equals 11.

Definition 21 (ee by universal property). e:=exp⁡(1)e := \exp(1). Equivalently, ee is the unique positive real such that the smooth solution ff of f′=ff' = f, f(0)=1f(0) = 1 satisfies f(1)=ef(1) = e. By Theorem 20, ee is well-defined independent of the construction of ℝ\mathbb{R}.

The Circle and the Number π\pi

Theorem 22 (Universal cover of S1S^1). The exponential map E:ℝ→S1,E(t):=(cos⁡t,sin⁡t),E \colon \mathbb{R}\to S^1, \qquad E(t) := (\cos t,\ \sin t), exhibits ℝ\mathbb{R} as the universal cover of S1S^1. The Coalgebraic Dual

Final Coalgebras and Codata

The dual of an initial algebra is a final coalgebra.

Definition 25 (FF-coalgebra). For an endofunctor F:𝒞→𝒞F \colon \mathcal{C} \to \mathcal{C}, an FF-coalgebra is a pair (X,γ)(X, \gamma) with γ:X→FX\gamma \colon X \to F X. A morphism of FF-coalgebras (X,γ)→(Y,δ)(X, \gamma) \to (Y, \delta) is h:X→Yh \colon X \to Y with δ∘h=Fh∘γ\delta \circ h = F h \circ \gamma.

Definition 26 (Final coalgebra). A final FF-coalgebra is a terminal object in the category of FF-coalgebras: an object (νF,out)(\nu F, \mathrm{out}) such that for every FF-coalgebra (X,γ)(X, \gamma) there is a unique morphism ana(γ):X→νF\mathrm{ana}(\gamma) \colon X \to \nu F.

The dual of Lambek’s lemma asserts that out:νF→F(νF)\mathrm{out} \colon \nu F \to F(\nu F) is an isomorphism. The unique morphism ana(γ)\mathrm{ana}(\gamma) is the anamorphism, the corecursion combinator.

Streams as Final Coalgebras

For a fixed alphabet AA, set FX:=A×XF X := A \times X.

Theorem 27 (Streams as final coalgebra). The set AℕA^{\mathbb{N}} of infinite sequences (streams) in AA is the final coalgebra of FX=A×XF X = A \times X, with out(s):=(s0,tail(s))\mathrm{out}(s) := (s_0, \mathrm{tail}(s)).

For any γ:X→A×X\gamma \colon X \to A \times X, ana(γ)\mathrm{ana}(\gamma) produces the stream of “observations” under repeated application of γ\gamma.

Inductive vs Coinductive Presentations of the Reals

The reals admit at least two HoTT-friendly presentations, corresponding to the two universal-property directions.

Inductive (Cauchy completion).

ℝ\mathbb{R} is constructed as the quotient of the set of Cauchy sequences ℚℕ\mathbb{Q}^{\mathbb{N}} by the relation (an)∼(bn)⇔|an−bn|→0(a_n) \sim (b_n) \iff |a_n - b_n| \to 0. This is presented as an initial-algebra-flavoured construction: ℝ\mathbb{R} is the smallest set closed under taking limits of Cauchy sequences.

Coinductive (digit streams).

For a chosen base b≥2b \ge 2 and digit set DD, the set of bb-adic digit streams DℕD^{\mathbb{N}} admits a quotient by “carry” equivalence; the quotient is [0,1]⊂ℝ[0, 1] \subset \mathbb{R}. The codomain has a natural coalgebraic description: it is the final coalgebra of X↦D×XX \mapsto D \times X (modulo the carry relation as a bisimulation quotient).

Remark 28 (Initial vs final tensions in ℝ\mathbb{R}). The Cauchy and digit-stream constructions are biased toward initial and final structures respectively. They produce isomorphic ordered fields by Theorem 19, but the constructions exhibit different computational content: Cauchy realizers carry an explicit modulus of convergence; stream realizers carry an explicit observation schedule. In Type II Turing computability, the two are interreducible but not isomorphic as effective structures . We take this as evidence that “ℝ\mathbb{R}” as a structural invariant lives above the choice of inductive vs coinductive presentation.

Bisimulation and the Coinduction Principle

For codata, equality is captured by bisimulation.

Definition 29 (Bisimulation). For an FF-coalgebra (X,γ)(X, \gamma), a bisimulation is a relation R⊆X×XR \subseteq X \times X such that there exists ρ:R→FR\rho \colon R \to F R making both projections π1,π2:R→X\pi_1, \pi_2 \colon R \to X coalgebra morphisms. Two elements x,y∈Xx, y \in X are bisimilar iff (x,y)∈R(x, y) \in R for some bisimulation RR.

Concrete case (FX=A×XF X = A \times X, streams). A relation R⊆X×XR \subseteq X \times X is a stream-bisimulation iff for every (x,y)∈R(x, y) \in R with γ(x)=(ax,x′)\gamma(x) = (a_x, x') and γ(y)=(ay,y′)\gamma(y) = (a_y, y'), we have ax=aya_x = a_y and (x′,y′)∈R(x', y') \in R. Two streams are bisimilar iff they are related by some such RR, equivalently iff they have equal head and bisimilar tails.

Theorem 30 (Coinduction principle). In the final coalgebra νF\nu F, two elements are equal if and only if they are bisimilar.

This is the coalgebraic analogue of the induction principle (Theorem 31 below): just as induction reduces equality of functions out of ℕ\mathbb{N} to equality of their basepoint-and-step components, coinduction reduces equality of elements in νF\nu F to the existence of a bisimulation.

Iteration, Primitive Recursion, Course-of-Values Recursion

We have shown that primitive recursion ([eq:prim-rec]) follows from iteration ([eq:rec-eqs]) (Proposition 13). Course-of-values recursion, where g(𝗌𝗎𝖼𝖼n)g(\mathsf{succ}n) may depend on all of g(0),g(1),…,g(n)g(0), g(1), \dots, g(n), is similarly derivable from iteration. We sketch the construction.

This pattern — bundling history into a state, iterating, and projecting — is the categorical reading of the standard “strong induction reduces to ordinary induction” move.

Higher Recursion Schemes

The pattern generalizes. For an endofunctor FF admitting an initial algebra μF\mu F, the unique morphism cata(α):μF→X\mathrm{cata}(\alpha) \colon \mu F \to X associated to an FF-algebra (X,α)(X, \alpha) is the catamorphism or fold. Dually, ana(γ):X→νF\mathrm{ana}(\gamma) \colon X \to \nu F is the anamorphism or unfold. Together they cover the basic recursion schemes of functional programming .

The Structuralist Critique: Up to Which Isomorphism?

The Concern

A familiar worry: a universal property determines its object only up to unique isomorphism. For numbers we say “ℕ\mathbb{N} is unique up to unique iso,” but in the category 𝑺𝒆𝒕\mathbf{Set} there are many concrete bijective copies of ℕ\mathbb{N} (von Neumann ordinals, Zermelo numerals, Church numerals, binary tries). The universal property guarantees a unique iso between any two such copies, but it does not select a particular set as “the” ℕ\mathbb{N}.

Lawvere’s Structuralist Response

Lawvere’s position , sharpened by Awodey , is that this is a feature, not a bug:

  1. Every property of mathematical interest is invariant under isomorphism.

  2. The universal property captures exactly the data needed to specify those properties.

  3. Asking “which ℕ\mathbb{N} is the real one?” commits a category mistake: one is reifying a label that the structure itself does not record.

This is the principle of structuralism: mathematical objects are positions in structures, identified by relational role.

What HoTT Adds (Forward Reference to Paper 5)

The structuralist response is satisfying philosophically but leaves a gap: in classical mathematics, isomorphism and equality are distinct relations, and one must constantly remember to “identify” isomorphic objects via an explicit transport. HoTT’s univalence axiom closes the gap: it asserts (A=B)≃(A≃B),\begin{equation} (A = B) \simeq (A \simeq B), \label{eq:univalence} \end{equation} making isomorphism and equality definitionally interchangeable.

In the universal-property perspective, this means: “the ℕ\mathbb{N}” is a perfectly good referent, because all candidates are not merely isomorphic but equal in the type-theoretic universe. The unique-iso ambiguity dissolves. We will develop this in Paper 5.

The “Which Iso?” Question Resolved

Even before univalence, the universal property already names a specific isomorphism between any two NNOs: the one given by the universal mapping property applied to the second NNO viewed as a pointed dynamical system. This is what is meant by “unique up to unique iso.” The deeper question is not just “which iso?” but “is the iso itself unique and structurally determined?” — and the answer is yes. The universal property itself names a single, canonical isomorphism between any two NNOs, with no further choice required: there is no wobble, no ambiguity, no Aut(N)(N) acting non-trivially on the comparison.

Proposition 34 (Canonical iso of NNOs). Let (N1,01,𝗌𝗎𝖼𝖼1)(N_1, 0_1, \mathsf{succ}_1) and (N2,02,𝗌𝗎𝖼𝖼2)(N_2, 0_2, \mathsf{succ}_2) be NNOs in 𝒞\mathcal{C}. The unique iso u:N1→N2u \colon N_1 \to N_2 provided by Lemma 4 is given explicitly by u=rec(02,𝗌𝗎𝖼𝖼2)u = \mathrm{rec}(0_2, \mathsf{succ}_2) (the recursion combinator from N1N_1 targeting N2N_2), with inverse rec(01,𝗌𝗎𝖼𝖼1)\mathrm{rec}(0_1, \mathsf{succ}_1). In particular, the automorphism group Aut(N)={idN}\mathrm{Aut}(N) = \{\mathrm{id}_N\} is trivial: any pointed-endo-system endomorphism of an NNO is the identity.

Proof. Apply Theorem 9 with target (N2,02,𝗌𝗎𝖼𝖼2)(N_2, 0_2, \mathsf{succ}_2) to obtain uu, and again with target (N1,01,𝗌𝗎𝖼𝖼1)(N_1, 0_1, \mathsf{succ}_1) to obtain a candidate inverse vv. By Lemma 4, uu and vv are mutually inverse. The triviality of Aut(N)\mathrm{Aut}(N) follows by taking N1=N2=NN_1 = N_2 = N: both uu and idN\mathrm{id}_N are pointed-endo-system endomorphisms of NN, hence equal by uniqueness in Theorem 9. ◻

Bridge to Yoneda

Universal Property as Functor Representation

A universal property always exhibits its object as the representing object of some functor. The key task is identifying which functor on which category, in a way that avoids the tautology of representing Hom(N,−)\mathrm{Hom}(N, -) by NN itself. The correct framing for the NNO uses the fact that initial objects represent constant singleton functors.

The Free–Forgetful Adjunction

The category 𝑷𝒕𝑬𝒏𝒅𝒐\mathbf{PtEndo} admits a forgetful functor U:𝑷𝒕𝑬𝒏𝒅𝒐→𝑺𝒆𝒕U \colon \mathbf{PtEndo}\to \mathbf{Set} sending (X,x0,f)↦X(X, x_0, f) \mapsto X and remembering only the underlying set. The functor UU is part of an adjunction F⊣U:𝑺𝒆𝒕⇄𝑷𝒕𝑬𝒏𝒅𝒐,F \dashv U \colon \mathbf{Set}\rightleftarrows \mathbf{PtEndo}, where F:𝑺𝒆𝒕→𝑷𝒕𝑬𝒏𝒅𝒐F \colon \mathbf{Set}\to \mathbf{PtEndo} is the free pointed-endo-system functor. The existence of FF follows from the adjoint functor theorem applied to the locally finitely presentable category 𝑷𝒕𝑬𝒏𝒅𝒐\mathbf{PtEndo}, or constructively from the fact that 𝑷𝒕𝑬𝒏𝒅𝒐\mathbf{PtEndo} is the category of algebras of the finitary endofunctor 1+(−)1 + (-) on 𝑺𝒆𝒕\mathbf{Set}. The defining adjunction isomorphism is, for every set AA and every pointed-endo-system (X,x0,f)(X, x_0, f): Hom𝑷𝒕𝑬𝒏𝒅𝒐(F(A),(X,x0,f))≅Hom𝑺𝒆𝒕(A,U(X,x0,f))=Hom𝑺𝒆𝒕(A,X).\begin{equation} \mathrm{Hom}_{\mathbf{PtEndo}}\bigl(F(A),\ (X, x_0, f)\bigr) \cong \mathrm{Hom}_{\mathbf{Set}}\bigl(A,\ U(X, x_0, f)\bigr) = \mathrm{Hom}_{\mathbf{Set}}(A, X). \label{eq:adjunction-iso} \end{equation} We use this isomorphism abstractly; the explicit formula for F(A)F(A) is not required for the argument that follows.

Theorem 35 (NNO as the free pointed-endo-system on the empty set). The natural numbers object (ℕ,0,𝗌𝗎𝖼𝖼)(\mathbb{N}, 0, \mathsf{succ}) is canonically isomorphic to the value of the free functor FF at the empty set: F(⌀)≅(ℕ,0,𝗌𝗎𝖼𝖼).\begin{equation} F(\varnothing) \cong (\mathbb{N}, 0, \mathsf{succ}). \label{eq:F-empty} \end{equation} Equivalently, ℕ\mathbb{N} is the initial object of 𝑷𝒕𝑬𝒏𝒅𝒐\mathbf{PtEndo}. Specializing the adjunction [eq:adjunction-iso] to A=⌀A = \varnothing recovers the universal property of the NNO: the right-hand side Hom𝑺𝒆𝒕(⌀,X)\mathrm{Hom}_{\mathbf{Set}}(\varnothing, X) is the singleton {!}\{!\} (the unique empty function), hence there is a unique morphism h:ℕ→Xh \colon \mathbb{N}\to X in 𝑷𝒕𝑬𝒏𝒅𝒐\mathbf{PtEndo}, which is exactly Theorem 9.

Proof. Left adjoints preserve initial objects. The initial object of 𝑺𝒆𝒕\mathbf{Set} is ⌀\varnothing. By the adjunction [eq:adjunction-iso] at A=⌀A = \varnothing, the hom-set Hom𝑷𝒕𝑬𝒏𝒅𝒐(F(⌀),(X,x0,f))\mathrm{Hom}_{\mathbf{PtEndo}}(F(\varnothing), (X, x_0, f)) is in natural bijection with the singleton Hom𝑺𝒆𝒕(⌀,X)\mathrm{Hom}_{\mathbf{Set}}(\varnothing, X), so F(⌀)F(\varnothing) is initial in 𝑷𝒕𝑬𝒏𝒅𝒐\mathbf{PtEndo}. By Theorem 9 the NNO is also initial in 𝑷𝒕𝑬𝒏𝒅𝒐\mathbf{PtEndo}, so by Lemma 4 there is a unique iso F(⌀)→∼(ℕ,0,𝗌𝗎𝖼𝖼)F(\varnothing) \xrightarrow{\sim} (\mathbb{N}, 0, \mathsf{succ}), which is the content of [eq:F-empty]. ◻

Initial Objects Represent the Constant Singleton Functor

The Yoneda content of initiality is that the NNO represents the constant singleton functor.

Corollary 36 (Representability of the NNO). There is a natural isomorphism of functors 𝑷𝒕𝑬𝒏𝒅𝒐→𝑺𝒆𝒕\mathbf{PtEndo}\to \mathbf{Set} Hom𝑷𝒕𝑬𝒏𝒅𝒐((ℕ,0,𝗌𝗎𝖼𝖼),−)≅Δ1,\begin{equation} \mathrm{Hom}_{\mathbf{PtEndo}}\bigl((\mathbb{N}, 0, \mathsf{succ}), -\bigr) \cong \Delta_1, \label{eq:nno-rep} \end{equation} where Δ1:𝑷𝒕𝑬𝒏𝒅𝒐→𝑺𝒆𝒕\Delta_1 \colon \mathbf{PtEndo}\to \mathbf{Set} is the constant functor at the singleton 11. By the Yoneda lemma, the universal element of this representation is the unique element of Δ1(ℕ)=1\Delta_1(\mathbb{N}) = 1, which is in bijection with idℕ∈Hom𝑷𝒕𝑬𝒏𝒅𝒐(ℕ,ℕ)\mathrm{id}_\mathbb{N}\in \mathrm{Hom}_{\mathbf{PtEndo}}(\mathbb{N}, \mathbb{N}).

Proof. By Theorem 35, the left-hand side of [eq:nno-rep] is, for every (X,x0,f)(X, x_0, f), a singleton set; this is exactly Δ1(X,x0,f)=1\Delta_1(X, x_0, f) = 1. Naturality is automatic from Δ1\Delta_1 being constant. ◻

Remark 37 (The free algebra on the singleton picks out elements). The companion fact, used in many treatments, is that the free pointed-endo-system on the singleton, F(1)F(1), represents the underlying-set functor U:𝑷𝒕𝑬𝒏𝒅𝒐→𝑺𝒆𝒕U \colon \mathbf{PtEndo}\to \mathbf{Set}: Hom𝑷𝒕𝑬𝒏𝒅𝒐(F(1),(X,x0,f))≅Hom𝑺𝒆𝒕(1,X)≅X=U(X,x0,f).\mathrm{Hom}_{\mathbf{PtEndo}}\bigl(F(1), (X, x_0, f)\bigr) \cong \mathrm{Hom}_{\mathbf{Set}}(1, X) \cong X = U(X, x_0, f). The NNO is the special case at A=⌀A = \varnothing. So the same adjunction [eq:adjunction-iso] explains both initiality (representability of Δ1\Delta_1) and freeness on a single generator (representability of UU), simply by varying the input set AA.

Reading the Universal Property Yonedically

The NNO universal property thus has a clean Yoneda interpretation:

  1. The free–forgetful adjunction F⊣U:𝑺𝒆𝒕⇄𝑷𝒕𝑬𝒏𝒅𝒐F \dashv U \colon \mathbf{Set}\rightleftarrows \mathbf{PtEndo} encodes the categorical infrastructure.

  2. The NNO is the value F(⌀)F(\varnothing) of the free functor at the empty set: it is the free pointed-endo-system with no extra generators.

  3. As an initial object, the NNO represents the constant singleton functor on 𝑷𝒕𝑬𝒏𝒅𝒐\mathbf{PtEndo} (Corollary 36).

  4. “58” is the position 𝗌𝗎𝖼𝖼58(0)∈ℕ\mathsf{succ}^{58}(0) \in \mathbb{N}, which under the unique morphism ℕ→(X,x0,f)\mathbb{N}\to (X, x_0, f) guaranteed by initiality maps to f58(x0)∈Xf^{58}(x_0) \in X. This position is invariant under all morphisms of 𝑷𝒕𝑬𝒏𝒅𝒐\mathbf{PtEndo} in the precise sense that any morphism (X,x0,f)→(Y,y0,g)(X, x_0, f) \to (Y, y_0, g) commutes with the unique maps from ℕ\mathbb{N}.

This recasting illuminates what universal properties are doing in general:

A universal property of an object UU in a category 𝒟\mathcal{D} exhibits UU as the value of a left adjoint at a distinguished object of some other category 𝒞\mathcal{C}, equivalently as the representing object for the functor Hom𝒞(c0,U(−))\mathrm{Hom}_{\mathcal{C}}(c_0, U(-)) for some c0∈𝒞c_0 \in \mathcal{C}. The Yoneda lemma supplies the universal element, namely the unit of the adjunction at c0c_0.

For initial objects, c0=⌀c_0 = \varnothing (in 𝑺𝒆𝒕\mathbf{Set}, or the initial object of 𝒞\mathcal{C} in general), and the represented functor is the constant singleton. For free objects on a generator, c0=1c_0 = 1, and the represented functor is the underlying-set functor. We will develop this perspective fully in Paper 4.

The Slogan

If Paper 02 said “a number is a set,” Paper 03 says “a number is a position in a universal diagram.” Paper 04 will say “a number is a representable functor.” Paper 05 will say “a number is a term up to path equivalence.” The Yoneda perspective is the next refinement, and it agrees with everything we have done.

Haskell Implementation

We accompany the mathematics with an executable Haskell library implementing the NNO universal property generically. The full code is in src/03-universal-property/; we summarize the design here.

The Iteration Combinator

The recursion combinator from Theorem 9 is the iteration combinator on Peano naturals. We define a generic Peano type, a helper to inject Int values into it, and the universal map:

-- | The natural numbers as the initial successor algebra.
data Nat = Z | S Nat deriving (Show, Eq)

-- | Inject an Int into Peano-style Nat (used for demonstrations).
peano :: Int -> Nat
peano n
  | n <= 0    = Z
  | otherwise = S (peano (n - 1))

-- | The universal property: unique map from Nat to any (X, x0, f).
--   nnoRec x0 f n = f^n x0
nnoRec :: x -> (x -> x) -> Nat -> x
nnoRec x0 _ Z     = x0
nnoRec x0 f (S n) = f (nnoRec x0 f n)

The two equations of nnoRec are exactly the two equations [eq:rec-eqs].

Generic Initial Algebras

We then implement the fold/cata combinator for arbitrary initial algebras:

newtype Mu f = Mu { unMu :: f (Mu f) }

cata :: Functor f => (f a -> a) -> Mu f -> a

Results

We collect the main results of the paper.

Theorem 38 (Main result — structural identity of ℕ\mathbb{N}). The natural numbers are determined up to unique isomorphism by the NNO universal property (Theorem 9). The notion “58” admits an encoding-independent meaning as the unique image of 𝗌𝗎𝖼𝖼58(0)\mathsf{succ}^{58}(0) under the canonical map into any pointed dynamical system. Two NNOs are connected by a unique iso (Lemma 4, Proposition 34); their copies of 58 correspond.

Theorem 39 (Main result — universal-property catalogue). The constants π\pi, ee, the structures ℤ,ℚ,ℝ,S1\mathbb{Z}, \mathbb{Q}, \mathbb{R}, S^1, and the functions exp⁡,sin⁡,cos\exp, \sin, \cos each admit a universal property in an appropriate category. These universal properties are encoding-independent and refer only to the categorical role of each object.

Theorem 40 (Main result — inductive/coinductive duality). The recursion principle (initial algebra) and the corecursion principle (final coalgebra) are dual instances of universal properties. Inductive presentations (e.g., Cauchy reals) and coinductive presentations (e.g., digit-stream reals) of the same structural object yield isomorphic structures by the universal property; the inductive/coinductive choice is a presentational rather than ontological matter.

Theorem 41 (Main result — representability). A universal property exhibits its object as the value of a left adjoint at a distinguished generator, equivalently as a representing object for a functor describable without reference to itself. For the NNO: ℕ=F(⌀)\mathbb{N}= F(\varnothing) is the value of the free functor F:𝑺𝒆𝒕→𝑷𝒕𝑬𝒏𝒅𝒐F \colon \mathbf{Set}\to \mathbf{PtEndo} (left adjoint to the forgetful UU) at the empty set, and as an initial object it represents the constant singleton functor on 𝑷𝒕𝑬𝒏𝒅𝒐\mathbf{PtEndo} (Theorem 35, Corollary 36). This is the bridge from the universal-property perspective (this paper) to the Yoneda perspective (Paper 4).

Discussion

Universal Properties as a Foundational Style

The universal-property style of definition has proliferated through 20th- and 21st-century mathematics: limits and colimits, free objects, adjoint functors, quotient constructions, classifying spaces. Each universal property packages an existence-and-uniqueness statement and, in cases where the relevant category is small enough, computational content (the unique morphism is constructively producible).

The universal-property perspective is more parsimonious than the set-theoretic perspective in two ways. First, it does not require choosing an encoding. Second, it scales: “define XX” becomes “state the universal property of XX,” which automatically transfers across categories whenever the universal property is satisfiable.

Limitations

Three limitations are worth noting:

Existence is not free.

The universal property of the NNO does not by itself guarantee that an NNO exists; one must verify it in the working category. ETCS adds existence as an axiom; topos theory verifies existence in many natural toposes .

Choice and impredicativity.

Some universal properties (e.g., the universal cover of a space) require background choice principles or impredicative quantification. The natural numbers do not, but the reals (via Dedekind completeness) implicitly assume powerset-like operations.

The which-iso question persists in classical mathematics.

As discussed in Section 8, classical mathematics maintains the distinction between isomorphism and equality. The universal property names a unique iso, but this remains a separate datum from equality. HoTT removes the distinction.

Connections to Computation

The recursion combinator rec\mathrm{rec} from Theorem 9 is the primitive recursion combinator of Gödel’s System T. The catamorphism on μF\mu F is the fold of functional programming. The anamorphism on νF\nu F is the unfold. Hylomorphisms (compositions cata∘ana\mathrm{cata} \circ \mathrm{ana}) capture divide-and-conquer recursion schemes. These computational artifacts are not coincidentally analogous to the categorical concepts; they are exactly the categorical concepts viewed under the lens of the Curry–Howard–Lambek correspondence .

To Paper 01 (Naive level).

The universal property dissolves the naive question “what is the symbol 58?” into the structural question “what does 58 do in any successor structure?”

To Paper 02 (Set-theoretic level).

The universal property explains why Benacerraf’s identification problem is not a problem: every encoding satisfying the universal property is canonically interconvertible with every other.

To Paper 04 (Yoneda).

The universal property is the rough-cut form of which Yoneda is the systematic theory: every universal property is a representation, and every representation can be read as a universal property.

To Paper 05 (HoTT).

Conclusion

We have presented the universal-property perspective on numbers, with the natural numbers object as the central case and ℤ,ℚ,ℝ,π,e,S1\mathbb{Z}, \mathbb{Q}, \mathbb{R}, \pi, e, S^1 as further instances. The universal property of the NNO is captured in a single equation pair (Theorem 9); from it follow recursion (Proposition 13), induction (Theorem 31), Lambek’s lemma (Lemma 6), the rigidity of the natural numbers (Lemma 4, Proposition 34), and the structural meaning of position 58 (Example 12, Theorem 38).

The coalgebraic dual provides codata: streams, infinite trees, and coinductive presentations of the reals. The two directions are complementary, not competing; the universal-property perspective regards them as inductive/coinductive presentations of one structural invariant.

(Paper 4.)

  2. How do higher inductive types in HoTT subsume universal properties for spaces (e.g., S1S^1 as a HIT with one point and one loop)? (Paper 5.)

The universal-property perspective has prepared the ground: numbers are positions in initial structures; everything else is encoding. Paper 4 will recast this in the language of representable functors, and Paper 5 will internalize it as definitional equality. The destination is univalent foundations; this paper is one rung of the ladder.
