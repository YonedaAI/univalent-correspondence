Introduction

Where we are in the series

This is the second paper in a six-paper series, plus a synthesis volume, exploring the question “what is a natural number?” at progressively higher levels of mathematical abstraction. Paper 01 considered the naive level, in which a number is treated as a primitive symbol or quantity, without further analysis. The naive level is the practical starting point of arithmetic and the inevitable surface of any numeral system, but it conflates syntax with semantics: the symbols “5858”, “LVIII”, and “||⋯|⏟58\underbrace{\vert\vert\cdots\vert}_{58}” all refer to the same number, but the naive level provides no machinery for distinguishing the numeral from the number it names.

The natural next step, taken in the foundational programs of the early twentieth century, is to reduce numbers to objects of a more basic theory. Frege attempted such a reduction to logic; Russell and Whitehead refined and elaborated it in Principia Mathematica ; Zermelo and later Fraenkel and Skolem developed the axiomatic set theory we now call ZFC. Within ZFC, John von Neumann gave a canonical recursive definition of the ordinals that, when restricted to the finite case, produces a particular set-theoretic implementation of the natural numbers. Standard expositions of the resulting machinery include Halmos , Suppes , Shoenfield , and Kunen .

The thesis of this paper

We make four claims:

  1. Set theory does provide a foundation for arithmetic. The von Neumann encoding inside ZFC produces a structure (ω,0,succ)(\omega, 0, \mathrm{succ}), where ω\omega is the smallest inductive set, on which addition, multiplication, and order can be defined recursively, and which satisfies the (second-order) Peano axioms.

  2. The encoding is not unique. Zermelo’s earlier encoding, 0=∅0 = \emptyset, n+1={n}n+1 = \{n\}, gives a different set-theoretic structure that nevertheless also satisfies the Peano axioms. Many other encodings exist. The encodings are mutually Peano-isomorphic but not mutually ZFC-isomorphic: as sets, they differ.

  3. The discrepancy generates junk theorems. In von Neumann’s encoding, 2∈32 \in 3 is true; in Zermelo’s, it is false. The question “is 22 an element of 33?” is meaningful inside ZFC for each encoding separately, but it is meaningless about the number 22 — it is an artifact of the encoding. Following Benacerraf , we conclude that numbers cannot be identified with sets.

  4. The structural content survives. What both encodings share is precisely the universal property of a Natural Number Object: they are both initial in a category of pointed sets with endomorphism. This is the structural content that Paper 03 will make explicit. The set-theoretic perspective is therefore not refuted but relocated: it is one implementation of a structural specification, not a definition.

Outline

Mathematical Framework: ZFC Reviewed

We work in classical first-order logic with equality and a single binary relation symbol ∈\in.

Axioms

Definition 1 (ZFC). The axioms of Zermelo–Fraenkel set theory with Choice are:

  1. Extensionality. ∀x∀y(∀z(z∈x↔z∈y)→x=y)\forall x\, \forall y\, \bigl(\forall z\,(z \in x \leftrightarrow z \in y) \to x = y\bigr).

  2. Pairing. ∀x∀y∃z∀w(w∈z↔w=x∨w=y)\forall x\, \forall y\, \exists z\, \forall w\,(w \in z \leftrightarrow w = x \vee w = y).

  3. Union. ∀x∃y∀z(z∈y↔∃w(w∈x∧z∈w))\forall x\, \exists y\, \forall z\,(z \in y \leftrightarrow \exists w\,(w \in x \wedge z \in w)).

  4. Power set. ∀x∃y∀z(z∈y↔z⊆x)\forall x\, \exists y\, \forall z\,(z \in y \leftrightarrow z \subseteq x).

  5. Separation schema. For each formula φ(z)\varphi(z) not involving yy, ∀x∃y∀z(z∈y↔z∈x∧φ(z))\forall x\, \exists y\, \forall z\,(z \in y \leftrightarrow z \in x \wedge \varphi(z)).

  6. Replacement schema. For each formula φ(x,y)\varphi(x,y) defining a class function FF, ∀A∃B∀y(y∈B↔∃x∈Aφ(x,y)).\forall A\, \exists B\, \forall y\, \bigl(y \in B \leftrightarrow \exists x \in A\,\, \varphi(x,y)\bigr).

  7. Infinity. ∃I(∅∈I∧∀x(x∈I→x∪{x}∈I))\exists I\,\bigl(\emptyset \in I \wedge \forall x\,(x \in I \to x \cup \{x\} \in I)\bigr).

  8. Foundation. ∀x(x≠∅→∃y∈x(y∩x=∅))\forall x\,(x \neq \emptyset \to \exists y \in x\,(y \cap x = \emptyset)).

  9. Choice. Every set of nonempty sets has a choice function.

Remark 2. The empty set ∅\emptyset is definable: by Separation applied to any set xx and the formula z≠zz \neq z, the resulting set has no elements. By Extensionality, this set is unique. (Inf) guarantees there is a set containing ∅\emptyset and closed under x↦x∪{x}x \mapsto x \cup \{x\}.

Axiom of Infinity, in detail

Definition 3 (Inductive set). A set II is inductive when ∅∈I\emptyset \in I and, for every x∈Ix \in I, x∪{x}∈Ix \cup \{x\} \in I. The axiom of infinity asserts that an inductive set exists.

Proposition 4 (Existence of ω\omega). There exists a unique smallest inductive set, denoted ω\omega. Equivalently, ω=⋂{I:I is inductive}\omega = \bigcap \{I : I \text{ is inductive}\} inside any inductive set, defined by Separation: ω={x∈I0:∀I(I inductive→x∈I)},\begin{equation} \omega = \{x \in I_0 : \forall I\,(I \text{ inductive} \to x \in I)\}, \end{equation} where I0I_0 is any fixed inductive set guaranteed by (Inf).

Proof. The class of inductive sets is nonempty by (Inf); the class-intersection is inductive (it contains ∅\emptyset since each inductive set does, and is closed under x↦x∪{x}x \mapsto x \cup \{x\} since each inductive set is). Separation applied to I0I_0 and the formula φ(x)≡∀I(Ind(I)→x∈I)\varphi(x) \equiv \forall I\,(\text{Ind}(I) \to x \in I) produces a set ω\omega with the required property. Uniqueness follows from Extensionality. ◻

Replacement, and what it buys us

The Separation schema only carves out subsets of an existing set; it cannot generate sets “larger” than what is already given. Replacement is strictly stronger: it asserts that the image of a set under a definable class function is a set. This is essential for transfinite recursion (and hence for the cumulative hierarchy of 7) and for the formal definition of operations such as addition by recursion on ω\omega.

Remark 5. ZFC without Replacement is Zermelo’s original 1908 system Z\mathrm{Z}, which is too weak for many constructions used in ordinary mathematics (Borel hierarchies, transfinite recursion past ω+ω\omega + \omega, etc.). For our purposes here — defining the natural numbers and their arithmetic — Replacement is the crucial axiom that lets us define functions by recursion.

Foundation and well-foundedness

Definition 6 (Well-founded relation). A relation RR on a class CC is well-founded when every nonempty subset S⊆CS \subseteq C has an RR-minimal element: an x∈Sx \in S with {y∈S:yRx}=∅\{y \in S : yRx\} = \emptyset.

Theorem 7. The Foundation axiom is equivalent (in ZF without Foundation) to the assertion that the membership relation ∈\in is well-founded on the universe of sets. In particular, there is no infinite descending ∈\in-chain ⋯∈x2∈x1∈x0\cdots \in x_2 \in x_1 \in x_0, no set xx with x∈xx \in x, and no cycle x0∈x1∈⋯∈xn∈x0x_0 \in x_1 \in \cdots \in x_n \in x_0.

Proof sketch. If x∈xx \in x, then {x}\{x\} has no ∈\in-minimal element, contradicting Foundation. The general statement is proved by considering the transitive closure of any candidate descending chain and applying Foundation to the resulting set. ◻

Foundation is what allows transfinite induction on the entire set-theoretic universe. It is also what makes the von Neumann encoding (in which nn is literally the set of its predecessors) compatible with the standard machinery: since each nn is a transitive set linearly ordered by ∈\in, Foundation guarantees we never run into pathologies like n∈nn \in n.

The von Neumann Encoding

Definition

Definition 8 (Successor). For any set xx, the successor of xx is x+:=x∪{x}x^+ := x \cup \{x\}.

Definition 9 (Von Neumann naturals). We define the von Neumann naturals as elements of ω\omega recursively: 0vN:=∅,(n+1)vN:=(nvN)+=nvN∪{nvN}.\begin{align} 0_{\mathrm{vN}} &:= \emptyset, \\ (n+1)_{\mathrm{vN}} &:= (n_{\mathrm{vN}})^+ = n_{\mathrm{vN}} \cup \{n_{\mathrm{vN}}\}. \end{align}

Unrolling this definition: 0=∅1={0}={∅}2={0,1}={∅,{∅}}3={0,1,2}={∅,{∅},{∅,{∅}}}⋮n={0,1,…,n−1}\begin{align*} 0 &= \emptyset \\ 1 &= \{0\} = \{\emptyset\} \\ 2 &= \{0, 1\} = \{\emptyset, \{\emptyset\}\} \\ 3 &= \{0, 1, 2\} = \{\emptyset, \{\emptyset\}, \{\emptyset, \{\emptyset\}\}\} \\ &\vdots \\ n &= \{0, 1, \ldots, n-1\} \end{align*}

Example 10. The number 5858, in von Neumann form, is the set of all smaller naturals: 58vN={0,1,2,…,57}.\begin{equation} 58_{\mathrm{vN}} = \{0, 1, 2, \ldots, 57\}. \end{equation} The set 5858 has exactly 5858 elements, and each of those elements is a set with strictly fewer elements. The total cumulative count of ∈\in-occurrences inside 5858 is large but finite (combinatorially, ∑k=057k=1653\sum_{k=0}^{57} k = 1653).

Membership and subset coincide

A signal feature of the von Neumann encoding is the conflation, at the finite level, of ∈\in and ⊆\subseteq.

Proposition 11. For all m,n∈ωm, n \in \omega, we have m∈nm \in n if and only if m⊊nm \subsetneq n.

Proof. We prove both directions by induction on nn.

(⇒)(\Rightarrow). If m∈nm \in n, then since nn is the set of its predecessors and is transitive (12 below), every element of mm is also an element of nn, hence m⊆nm \subseteq n. Strict inclusion follows from m∈nm \in n and m∉mm \notin m (Foundation, 7).

(⇐)(\Leftarrow). If m⊊nm \subsetneq n, pick any k∈n\mk \in n \setminus m of minimal ∈\in-rank. Then k⊆mk \subseteq m (by minimality), and k=mk = m follows from extensionality together with m⊆nm \subseteq n and the definition of nn as the set of its predecessors. Hence m=k∈nm = k \in n. ◻

Proposition 12 (Transitivity of naturals). Each n∈ωn \in \omega is a transitive set: if x∈nx \in n, then x⊆nx \subseteq n.

Proof. By induction. 0=∅0 = \emptyset is vacuously transitive. Assume nn is transitive and let x∈n+=n∪{n}x \in n^+ = n \cup \{n\}. If x=nx = n, then x=n⊆n+x = n \subseteq n^+ since n∪{n}⊇nn \cup \{n\} \supseteq n. If x∈nx \in n, then by the inductive hypothesis x⊆n⊆n+x \subseteq n \subseteq n^+. ◻

Example 13. 2∈32 \in 3 holds in von Neumann: 3={0,1,2}3 = \{0,1,2\}, so 22 is literally one of the three elements of 33. Equivalently, 2={0,1}⊊{0,1,2}=32 = \{0,1\} \subsetneq \{0,1,2\} = 3. This is the kind of statement that, by 11, is equivalent in von Neumann to the order 2<32 < 3.

Peano axioms for the von Neumann naturals

Theorem 14. The structure (ω,0vN,(⋅)+)(\omega, 0_{\mathrm{vN}}, (\cdot)^+) satisfies the Peano axioms:

  1. 0vN∈ω0_{\mathrm{vN}} \in \omega.

  2. For all n∈ωn \in \omega, n+∈ωn^+ \in \omega.

  3. For all n∈ωn \in \omega, n+≠0vNn^+ \neq 0_{\mathrm{vN}}.

  4. For all m,n∈ωm,n \in \omega, if m+=n+m^+ = n^+ then m=nm = n.

  5. (Induction) For every set S⊆ωS \subseteq \omega, if 0vN∈S0_{\mathrm{vN}} \in S and SS is closed under (⋅)+(\cdot)^+, then S=ωS = \omega.

Proof. (1) and (2) are immediate from the definition of ω\omega as the smallest inductive set.

(3) If n+=∅n^+ = \emptyset, then n∪{n}=∅n \cup \{n\} = \emptyset. But n∈n∪{n}n \in n \cup \{n\}, contradiction.

(4) Suppose m+=n+m^+ = n^+, i.e. m∪{m}=n∪{n}m \cup \{m\} = n \cup \{n\}. Then m∈n+m \in n^+ and n∈m+n \in m^+. By 11 (applied via ∈\in-trichotomy), if m≠nm \neq n then either m∈nm \in n and n∈mn \in m, which contradicts Foundation, or one is a proper subset of the other; combined with m∈n+m \in n^+ and n∈m+n \in m^+ this forces m=nm = n.

(5) Let S⊆ωS \subseteq \omega be inductive (containing 00 and closed under (⋅)+(\cdot)^+). Then SS itself is an inductive set, so ω⊆S\omega \subseteq S by minimality of ω\omega. The reverse inclusion is by hypothesis. ◻

Zermelo’s Encoding

Ernst Zermelo, in his 1908 axiomatization , used a different recursive definition of the natural numbers.

Definition 15 (Zermelo naturals). We define the Zermelo naturals recursively: 0Z:=∅,(n+1)Z:={nZ}.\begin{align} 0_{\mathrm{Z}} &:= \emptyset, \\ (n+1)_{\mathrm{Z}} &:= \{n_{\mathrm{Z}}\}. \end{align}

Unrolling: 0Z=∅1Z={∅}2Z={{∅}}3Z={{{∅}}}⋮nZ={{⋯{⏟n∅}⋯}}⏟n\begin{align*} 0_{\mathrm{Z}} &= \emptyset \\ 1_{\mathrm{Z}} &= \{\emptyset\} \\ 2_{\mathrm{Z}} &= \{\{\emptyset\}\} \\ 3_{\mathrm{Z}} &= \{\{\{\emptyset\}\}\} \\ &\vdots \\ n_{\mathrm{Z}} &= \underbrace{\{\{\cdots\{}_{n}\emptyset\underbrace{\}\cdots\}\}}_{n} \end{align*}

Example 16. 58Z58_{\mathrm{Z}} is a chain of 5858 nested singletons: 58Z={{⋯{⏟58∅}⋯}}⏟58.\begin{equation} 58_{\mathrm{Z}} = \underbrace{\{\{\cdots\{}_{58}\emptyset\underbrace{\}\cdots\}\}}_{58}. \end{equation} Crucially, 58Z58_{\mathrm{Z}} has exactly one element (namely 57Z57_{\mathrm{Z}}), not 5858. The cardinality of the encoding does not match the natural number it encodes.

Remark 19 (Junk). We call statements like “2∈32 \in 3” junk theorems (or junk falsehoods). They have a determinate truth value in ZFC under any fixed encoding, but they reflect choices made by the encoder, not facts about the natural numbers as such. The number 22 does not stand in any intrinsic membership relation to the number 33. The membership question is well-formed only because we have committed to representing numbers as sets; under a different commitment, the answer changes.

Iso as Peano models, not as sets

Theorem 22. There is a unique bijection Φ:ω→ωZ\Phi: \omega \to \omega_{\mathrm{Z}} satisfying Φ(0vN)=0Z\Phi(0_{\mathrm{vN}}) = 0_{\mathrm{Z}} and Φ(n+)={Φ(n)}\Phi(n^+) = \{\Phi(n)\} for all nn. The bijection Φ\Phi is an isomorphism of Peano structures.

Proof. Existence and uniqueness of Φ\Phi follow from the recursion theorem on ω\omega, which we may take as a consequence of Replacement and the universal property of ω\omega as the initial Peano structure (see 8). The bijection is automatic since both encodings are initial. ◻

Remark 23. Φ\Phi is an isomorphism of Peano structures (i.e. of (N,0,S)(N, 0, S)-algebras), but it is not an isomorphism of sets in any deeper sense: 2vN={0,1}2_{\mathrm{vN}} = \{0,1\} has two elements while 2Z={{0}}2_{\mathrm{Z}} = \{\{0\}\} has one. Benacerraf and the Identification Problem

The argument

In “What Numbers Could Not Be” (1965), Paul Benacerraf considers two children, Ernie and Johnny, each taught arithmetic via a different set-theoretic encoding (one Zermelo, one von Neumann). Each child’s arithmetic is correct: addition, multiplication, and order all behave normally. Yet they disagree on whether 3∈173 \in 17 (true in von Neumann, false in Zermelo). Benacerraf argues:

  1. Both encodings satisfy the structural conditions we use to identify “the natural numbers.”

  2. Neither has any feature, available within set theory, that privileges it as “the truth.”

  3. If numbers are sets, then there must be a fact of the matter as to which sets they are. Since there is no such fact, numbers are not sets.

The conclusion — numbers are not sets — is the central result of the paper, and it is the negative half of the structuralist thesis. Benacerraf revisits related epistemological worries about the relationship between mathematical truth and platonist ontology in ; the mature philosophical articulation of structuralism on the positive side is due to Shapiro . This positive half (numbers are positions in structures) is what motivates the move to universal properties (Paper 03), Yoneda (Paper 04), HoTT (Paper 05) — whose canonical reference is the HoTT Book — and structural categorical foundations (Paper 06) in the rest of the series.

Formalization of Benacerraf’s argument

We can formalize the argument as follows. Let 𝒫\mathcal{P} denote the class of all Peano structures (sets XX equipped with 0X∈X0_X \in X and SX:X→XS_X: X \to X satisfying the Peano axioms).

Theorem 24 (No canonical Peano structure). There is no formula φ(X,0,S)\varphi(X, 0, S) in the language of ZFC such that, in every model of ZFC, φ\varphi uniquely identifies a Peano structure (X,0,S)(X, 0, S) with the property that any other Peano structure (Y,0′,S′)(Y, 0', S') is isomorphic to (X,0,S)(X, 0, S) via a unique isomorphism, and φ\varphi uses no choices of encoding.

Sketch. Any formula φ\varphi identifying a particular Peano structure must specify the carrier set, which means committing to a particular extension inside the cumulative hierarchy. Since there are many extensionally distinct sets that satisfy any given Peano axiomatization (by the methods of 3 and 4), no such formula is encoding-neutral. The most one can ask of φ\varphi is that it identifies a Peano structure up to isomorphism, but this is already given by the structural description of 8, and is precisely what motivates abandoning set-level identification. ◻

Junk theorems formalized

Definition 25 (Junk theorem). Let TT be a theory and EE an encoding of a structure (N,0,S)(N, 0, S) inside TT (i.e. a translation of the language of (N,0,S)(N, 0, S) into the language of TT). A formula ψ\psi in the language of TT involving the encoded constants is a junk theorem relative to EE if ψ\psi is provable in TT but not invariant under reasonable changes of encoding E→E′E \to E'.

Example 26. The formula ψ:=1∈3\psi := 1 \in 3 is a junk theorem of ZFC under the von Neumann encoding: it is provable (since 3vN={0,1,2}3_{\mathrm{vN}} = \{0,1,2\}), but its truth value is reversed under the Zermelo encoding (since 3Z={2Z}3_{\mathrm{Z}} = \{2_{\mathrm{Z}}\}, and 1Z={∅}≠2Z1_{\mathrm{Z}} = \{\emptyset\} \neq 2_{\mathrm{Z}}). By contrast, ψ:=S(2)=3\psi := S(2) = 3 is not a junk theorem: it holds in every Peano-isomorphic encoding. The membership relation across non-adjacent ordinals is exactly the kind of fact that is forced by the encoding rather than by the structure.

Remark 27 (Junk vs. structural). A statement is structural when its truth value is preserved by every isomorphism of Peano structures; junk theorems are precisely the non-structural statements that nevertheless have determinate truth values once an encoding is fixed. The set-theoretic perspective generates many junk theorems; the categorical and HoTT perspectives generate none, by construction.

Arithmetic Operations as Recursive Definitions

We now show that the von Neumann encoding supports a full development of arithmetic. The development is entirely standard; we include it for the record and to demonstrate concretely that the structural Peano axioms are sufficient (the additional set-theoretic content is not needed). The same definitions, mutatis mutandis, apply to the Zermelo encoding.

The recursion theorem

Theorem 28 (Recursion on ω\omega). Let XX be any set, a∈Xa \in X a base point, and g:X→Xg: X \to X a function. Then there is a unique function f:ω→Xf: \omega \to X satisfying f(0)=a,f(n+)=g(f(n))for all n∈ω.\begin{align} f(0) &= a, \\ f(n^+) &= g(f(n)) \quad \text{for all } n \in \omega. \end{align}

Proof sketch. Define ff as the union of all finite attempts — functions h:m→Xh: m \to X with m∈ωm \in \omega satisfying the recursion equations on their domain. Replacement and Separation guarantee that the union of all attempts is a set; induction guarantees that ff is well-defined and total on ω\omega. ◻

Addition

Definition 29 (Addition). For m∈ωm \in \omega, define m+(⋅):ω→ωm + (\cdot) : \omega \to \omega by recursion: m+0:=m,m+n+:=(m+n)+.\begin{align} m + 0 &:= m, \\ m + n^+ &:= (m + n)^+. \end{align}

Proposition 30 (Properties of addition). For all m,n,k∈ωm, n, k \in \omega:

  1. 0+n=n0 + n = n (left identity).

  2. (m+n)+k=m+(n+k)(m + n) + k = m + (n + k) (associativity).

  3. m+n=n+mm + n = n + m (commutativity).

Proof. (1) Induct on nn. Base: 0+0=00 + 0 = 0. Step: assume 0+n=n0 + n = n; then 0+n+=(0+n)+=n+0 + n^+ = (0 + n)^+ = n^+.

(2) Induct on kk. Base: (m+n)+0=m+n=m+(n+0)(m+n) + 0 = m + n = m + (n + 0). Step: (m+n)+k+=((m+n)+k)+=(m+(n+k))+=m+(n+k)+=m+(n+k+)(m+n) + k^+ = ((m+n) + k)^+ = (m + (n+k))^+ = m + (n+k)^+ = m + (n + k^+).

(3) Commutativity is the deepest of the three properties; it requires a nested double induction. We prove it by establishing two auxiliary lemmas first.

Lemma A: 0+n=n+0=n0 + n = n + 0 = n. The right equation is 29; the left is part (1).

Lemma B: m++n=(m+n)+m^+ + n = (m + n)^+. Induct on nn. Base: m++0=m+=(m+0)+m^+ + 0 = m^+ = (m + 0)^+. Step: assuming m++n=(m+n)+m^+ + n = (m + n)^+, m++n+=(m++n)+=((m+n)+)+=(m+n+)+,m^+ + n^+ = (m^+ + n)^+ = ((m+n)^+)^+ = (m + n^+)^+, where the last step uses 29.

Main induction (on nn). Base (n=0n = 0): m+0=m=0+mm + 0 = m = 0 + m by Lemma A.

Step: assume m+n=n+mm + n = n + m for all mm. Then for any fixed mm, $$\begin{align*} m + n^+ &= (m + n)^+ &&\text{(\Cref{def:add})} \\ &= (n + m)^+ &&\text{(inductive hypothesis)} \\ &= n^+ + m &&\text{(Lemma B with $m, n$ swapped)}, \end{align*}$$ which establishes m+n+=n++mm + n^+ = n^+ + m and completes the induction on nn. ◻

Multiplication

Definition 31 (Multiplication). For m∈ωm \in \omega, define m⋅(⋅):ω→ωm \cdot (\cdot): \omega \to \omega by: m⋅0:=0,m⋅n+:=m⋅n+m.\begin{align} m \cdot 0 &:= 0, \\ m \cdot n^+ &:= m \cdot n + m. \end{align}

Proposition 32. Multiplication is associative, commutative, has 1:=0+1 := 0^+ as a two-sided identity, and distributes over addition.

Proof. Standard inductions on ω\omega, parallel to those for addition. The distributivity proof goes by induction on the right argument: m⋅(n+0)=m⋅n=m⋅n+0=m⋅n+m⋅0m \cdot (n + 0) = m \cdot n = m \cdot n + 0 = m \cdot n + m \cdot 0, and m⋅(n+k+)=m⋅(n+k)+=m⋅(n+k)+m=m⋅n+m⋅k+m=m⋅n+m⋅k+m \cdot (n + k^+) = m \cdot (n + k)^+ = m \cdot (n+k) + m = m \cdot n + m \cdot k + m = m \cdot n + m \cdot k^+. ◻

Order

Definition 33 (Order). For m,n∈ωm, n \in \omega, write m<nm < n iff m∈nm \in n, and m≤nm \leq n iff m∈n∨m=nm \in n \vee m = n.

Proof. Trichotomy is by induction. Transitivity follows from 12: if m∈nm \in n and n∈kn \in k, then n⊆kn \subseteq k by transitivity of kk, so m∈km \in k. Irreflexivity is Foundation. ◻

The Cumulative Hierarchy

Definition by transfinite recursion

Definition 36 (Cumulative hierarchy). By transfinite recursion on the class of ordinals Ord\mathrm{Ord}, define V0:=∅,Vα+1:=𝒫(Vα),Vλ:=⋃β<λVβ(λ limit).\begin{align} V_0 &:= \emptyset, \\ V_{\alpha+1} &:= \mathcal{P}(V_\alpha), \\ V_\lambda &:= \bigcup_{\beta < \lambda} V_\beta \quad (\lambda \text{ limit}). \end{align} The class V:=⋃α∈OrdVαV := \bigcup_{\alpha \in \mathrm{Ord}} V_\alpha is the universe of sets.

Theorem 37 (ZFC sees only VV). In ZFC, every set xx belongs to some VαV_\alpha. Hence V=UniverseV = \mathrm{Universe}.

Sketch. Define the rank function rk:V→Ord\mathrm{rk}: V \to \mathrm{Ord} by transfinite recursion: rk(x):=sup⁡{rk(y)+1:y∈x}\mathrm{rk}(x) := \sup\{\mathrm{rk}(y) + 1 : y \in x\}. Foundation guarantees this recursion terminates. Then x∈Vrk(x)+1x \in V_{\mathrm{rk}(x) + 1}. ◻

Ranks of natural numbers

Proposition 38. For each n∈ωn \in \omega:

  1. rk(nvN)=n\mathrm{rk}(n_{\mathrm{vN}}) = n.

  2. rk(nZ)=n\mathrm{rk}(n_{\mathrm{Z}}) = n.

Proof. For von Neumann: rk(0)=0\mathrm{rk}(0) = 0, and inductively rk(n+)=sup⁡{rk(k)+1:k∈n+}=sup⁡{k+1:k≤n}=n+1\mathrm{rk}(n^+) = \sup\{\mathrm{rk}(k) + 1 : k \in n^+\} = \sup\{k+1 : k \leq n\} = n+1.

For Zermelo: rk({nZ})=rk(nZ)+1\mathrm{rk}(\{n_{\mathrm{Z}}\}) = \mathrm{rk}(n_{\mathrm{Z}}) + 1, and the result follows by induction. ◻

Remark 39. Both encodings produce naturals at the same rank in VV, but they live at different positions within Vn+1V_{n+1}. Von Neumann’s nn is the “flat” set {0,1,…,n−1}\{0,1,\ldots,n-1\}; Zermelo’s is the “tall thin” nested singleton.

ω\omega as the first limit ordinal

Theorem 40. ω∈Vω+1\omega \in V_{\omega+1} but ω∉Vω\omega \notin V_\omega. The set ω\omega is the smallest infinite ordinal: the first ordinal that is not a successor.

Proof. ω=⋃n∈ωn\omega = \bigcup_{n \in \omega} n, so each n∈Vn⊆Vωn \in V_n \subseteq V_\omega, hence ω⊆Vω\omega \subseteq V_\omega and ω∈Vω+1\omega \in V_{\omega+1}. To see ω∉Vω\omega \notin V_\omega: every element of VωV_\omega has finite rank, and rk(ω)=ω\mathrm{rk}(\omega) = \omega. ◻

Visualizing the hierarchy

A Representation Theorem

We extract from the foregoing the following algebraic statement, which expresses the structural fact that all encodings of the natural numbers are mutually isomorphic.

Definition 41 (Successor structure). A successor structure is a triple (X,x0,s)(X, x_0, s) where XX is a set, x0∈Xx_0 \in X, and s:X→Xs: X \to X. A morphism (X,x0,s)→(Y,y0,t)(X, x_0, s) \to (Y, y_0, t) is a function f:X→Yf: X \to Y with f(x0)=y0f(x_0) = y_0 and f∘s=t∘ff \circ s = t \circ f.

Definition 42 (Initial Peano structure). A successor structure (N,0,S)(N, 0, S) is initial (or a Peano structure) when, for every successor structure (X,x0,s)(X, x_0, s), there exists a unique morphism h:(N,0,S)→(X,x0,s)h: (N, 0, S) \to (X, x_0, s).

Theorem 43 (Initiality of ω\omega). (ω,0vN,(⋅)+)(\omega, 0_{\mathrm{vN}}, (\cdot)^+) is an initial Peano structure. Equivalently: for every set XX, every a∈Xa \in X, and every g:X→Xg: X \to X, there is a unique f:ω→Xf: \omega \to X with f(0)=af(0) = a and f(n+)=g(f(n))f(n^+) = g(f(n)). This ff is the recursion theorem (28).

Proof. This is precisely the recursion theorem, recast as initiality. Existence is by recursion on ω\omega; uniqueness is by induction. ◻

Theorem 44 (Uniqueness of Peano structures). If (N,0,S)(N, 0, S) and (N′,0′,S′)(N', 0', S') are both initial Peano structures, then there is a unique isomorphism between them.

Proof. Universal property: by initiality of (N,0,S)(N, 0, S), there is a unique h:N→N′h: N \to N'. By initiality of (N′,0′,S′)(N', 0', S'), there is a unique h′:N′→Nh': N' \to N. The composites h′∘hh' \circ h and h∘h′h \circ h' are endomorphisms of an initial structure, hence equal to the identity by uniqueness. ◻

Corollary 45. The von Neumann and Zermelo successor structures are isomorphic via a unique isomorphism, namely Φ\Phi of 22.

This is the structural content that survives Benacerraf’s critique. Both encodings are initial Peano structures; they are therefore canonically identified at the structural level. What differs is the encoding-specific data: cardinalities of the carrier sets at each level, membership relations, etc. The structural mathematics survives; the junk does not.

Bridge to Structuralism

Reading the structuralist response

The standard structuralist response to Benacerraf is to assert that “numbers are positions in structures, not particular objects.” Within the set-theoretic perspective alone, this assertion is hard to formalize: the structuralist seems to be invoking abstract structures that are not themselves sets.

What Paper 03 will show is that the universal property of an initial Peano structure is precisely the structuralist content the response was gesturing at. [thm:initiality,thm:uniqueness] together say:

There is a structure (the initial successor structure), unique up to unique isomorphism, defined entirely by its relationships with all other successor structures. This structure can be implemented in many ways inside ZFC; no implementation is canonical, but every implementation is equivalent to every other in the only way that matters — via the universal mapping property.

The set-theoretic perspective relocated

We can therefore retract the temptation to read the von Neumann encoding as ontology. The set-theoretic perspective is best understood as realizing or instantiating a structural specification, not as defining what the natural numbers are. The number 5858 is not the set {0,1,…,57}\{0,1,\ldots,57\}; rather, the set {0,1,…,57}\{0,1,\ldots,57\} is one particular ZFC-implementation of the position 5858 inside the abstract initial successor structure.

What set theory still gets right

Despite Benacerraf’s critique, the set-theoretic perspective is not worthless. It contributes:

  • Existence. Without an existence proof, the universal property of 42 is mere stipulation. Set theory shows that an initial Peano structure exists (the axiom of infinity gives one), so the specification is not vacuous.

  • A working metatheory. ZFC is rich enough to prove the recursion theorem, define addition and multiplication, prove the soundness of induction, and develop most of ordinary mathematics. The structural specification needs a host theory; ZFC is one (other choices: ETCS, IZF, HoTT itself).

  • Cumulative hierarchy as a model of well-foundedness. The VαV_\alpha stratification, while encoding-relative in detail, captures the genuine structural fact that any well-founded iteration of some generative operation eventually exhausts what can be reached.

  • Decidable arithmetic. The recursive definitions of ++, ⋅\cdot, and << are decidable; the resulting theory of arithmetic is Σ1\Sigma_1-complete and useful in practice.

What set theory misses

  • Structural identity. Set-theoretic equality is extensional: two sets are equal iff they have the same elements. There is no native notion of “equal as Peano structures” inside ZFC; isomorphism must be defined externally and tracked manually.

  • Encoding-independence. As shown, the set-theoretic statement “2∈32 \in 3” is encoding-relative. The structural statement “succ(succ(0))<succ3(0)\mathrm{succ}(\mathrm{succ}(0)) < \mathrm{succ}^3(0)” is not.

  • Universal properties as theorems, not metatheorems. In ZFC, “ω\omega is initial” is a theorem, but the very statement of initiality requires quantifying over class-many successor structures (properly handled, but unwieldy). In a categorical or type-theoretic foundation, the universal property is the definition and the existence/uniqueness are immediate consequences.

  • Univalence. ZFC has no analog of the univalence axiom of HoTT; it cannot internalize “isomorphic structures are the same” as a theorem of the system. The closest one comes is to work systematically “up to isomorphism,” which is a metatheoretic discipline rather than a feature of the formalism.

Haskell Implementation

The accompanying Haskell code (src/02-set-theoretic/) implements both encodings, defines a common Peano interface they both satisfy, and provides a converter realizing the canonical isomorphism Φ\Phi of 22.

The hereditarily finite set type

We model pure sets as a recursive datatype:

-- A finite set built recursively from the empty set.
-- Extensional equality is enforced in the Eq instance by sorting
-- and deduplicating the underlying list before comparison; a
-- canonical-form helper (omitted here) makes this efficient.
newtype HFSet = HFSet { elements :: [HFSet] }
  deriving (Show)

instance Ord HFSet where
  compare (HFSet xs) (HFSet ys) =
    compare (sort xs) (sort ys)

instance Eq HFSet where
  HFSet xs == HFSet ys = sort xs == sort ys

emptySet :: HFSet
emptySet = HFSet []

Von Neumann encoding

-- Successor: x |-> x \cup {x}
vnSucc :: HFSet -> HFSet
vnSucc x = HFSet (elements x ++ [x])

vnEncode :: Int -> HFSet
vnEncode 0 = emptySet
vnEncode n = vnSucc (vnEncode (n - 1))

Zermelo encoding

-- Successor: x |-> {x}
zSucc :: HFSet -> HFSet
zSucc x = HFSet [x]

zEncode :: Int -> HFSet
zEncode 0 = emptySet
zEncode n = zSucc (zEncode (n - 1))

Junk theorems made explicit

The Haskell code also includes a function that demonstrates a junk theorem empirically:

-- Demonstrate that "1 in 3" depends on encoding.
junkTheorem :: IO ()
junkTheorem = do
  let one_vN  = vnEncode 1; three_vN = vnEncode 3
  let one_Z   = zEncode  1; three_Z  = zEncode  3
  putStrLn $ "vN: 1 in 3 = " ++ show (one_vN `inVN` three_vN)
  putStrLn $ "Z:  1 in 3 = " ++ show (one_Z  `inZ`  three_Z)
  -- Output:
  --   vN: 1 in 3 = True   (since 3_vN = {0,1,2})
  --   Z:  1 in 3 = False  (since 3_Z  = {2}; only 2 is in it)

This program is a working witness that the encoding is genuinely choosing between distinct sets, not merely between distinct notations.

Discussion

Comparison with logicism and constructivism

The set-theoretic foundationalism we have presented is closer to Russell–Whitehead’s logicist program than to a strict constructivism. The naturals are obtained by an unbounded inductive process whose existence is asserted by an axiom (Infinity). This is not predicatively justified in a strict sense (the inductive set is defined as the intersection of all inductive sets, a quantification over a complete totality), but it is mainstream classical mathematics.

Constructive alternatives — IZF, CZF, Martin-Löf type theory — avoid this by treating ℕ\mathbb{N} as primitive (an inductive type) rather than as a derived set. Paper 05 treats this perspective via HoTT.

Comparison with categorical foundations

Lawvere’s Elementary Theory of the Category of Sets (ETCS, ) gives a categorical axiomatization of the category 𝑺𝒆𝒕\mathbf{Set} that is bi-interpretable with bounded Zermelo set theory plus replacement (McLarty ). In ETCS, the natural number object is specified by its universal property (Paper 03’s level), not by a particular encoding. ETCS is therefore structurally cleaner than ZFC for foundational purposes, while equally adequate for ordinary mathematics. The underlying functorial-semantics machinery is developed in Lawvere , and the broader topos-theoretic context is treated by Mac Lane and Moerdijk .

Limitations of this paper

We have not discussed:

  • Large cardinal hypotheses. Inaccessible, measurable, etc. cardinals refine the cumulative hierarchy beyond the ordinals reachable in vanilla ZFC. They are foundational for set theory but tangential to the natural-numbers question.

  • The constructible universe LL. Gödel’s inner model exhibits a minimal model of ZFC; the natural numbers are the same in LL as in VV, so this is not pertinent.

  • Forcing. Independence results do not affect our development.

  • Alternative set theories. NF (Quine), KP (Kripke–Platek), and other systems give variant encodings; we have considered only ZFC.

What this paper has accomplished

We have presented two distinct set-theoretic encodings of the natural numbers (von Neumann ordinals and Zermelo nested singletons), shown that both satisfy the Peano axioms, and identified the encoding-relative facts (“junk theorems”) that distinguish them. We have located the natural numbers in the cumulative hierarchy and proven a representation theorem identifying all initial Peano structures up to unique isomorphism. We have described the structuralist response (numbers are positions, not sets) and pointed forward to the universal-property and HoTT formulations of that response.

Conclusion

The set-theoretic perspective gives us ω\omega via the axiom of infinity and the recursive successor x↦x∪{x}x \mapsto x \cup \{x\}. It supports a full arithmetic, fits cleanly into the cumulative hierarchy, and makes the natural numbers concrete: the number 5858 is the set {0,1,…,57}\{0,1,\ldots,57\} — in the von Neumann encoding.

But the qualifier “in the von Neumann encoding” is fatal to the foundationalist ambition. Zermelo’s encoding is equally valid; so is the Peano-axiomatic specification with no encoding at all; and so are the many other encodings one could devise. Each gives a working arithmetic that satisfies the Peano axioms, but each disagrees with the others on internal questions about element-of, cardinality, and rank. These disagreements are not pathologies; they are evidence that no encoding gives the natural numbers — only one realization of them.

What we have learned is that the natural numbers are characterized structurally, not extensionally. This characterization is the universal property of an initial Peano structure (42), and its sharpening into the categorical Natural Number Object will occupy Paper 03. The set-theoretic perspective remains useful as a metatheory and an existence proof, but the identity of the natural numbers themselves lives at one level of abstraction higher.
